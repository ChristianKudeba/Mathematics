# P16.1.b.2. Prime-by-prime decoupling of $\mathcal{B}(N)$ via Mellin

## Status

Second sub-chunk of chunk **P16.1.b** (continuation of `P16.1.b.1-outer-sum-reformulation.md`). Implements **Approach A** (Mellin envelope) from P16.1.b.1 §6. The deliverable is the explicit decoupled-over-primes formula

$$
\mathcal{B}(N) \;\le\; \mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) \;=\; \frac{1}{(2\pi i)^4}\int_{(c)^4} N^{s_1+s_2+s_3+s_4}\cdot \widehat{\Psi}(\vec s)\cdot\mathcal{D}_F^{\ast}(\vec s)\cdot \mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)\,d^4 s
$$

with $\mathcal{D}_F^{\ast}=\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}-1$, $\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s)=\prod_{p\equiv 1(4)} L_p(\vec s)$, $\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)=\prod_{p\mid N^2+1}M_p(\vec s)$, and the explicit local factors / closed forms below. Chunk b.3 will perform pole-structure / contour analysis to extract $\mathcal{B}(N)\le C\,K(N)^2(\log N)^B$.

**This file proves / states:**

1. **Proposition 11 (Mellin smoothing).** For any $\Psi:\mathbb{R}_{>0}\to[0,1]$ with $\Psi=1$ on $(0,1]$, $\Psi=0$ on $[2,\infty)$, $\Psi\ge \mathbf 1_{(0,1]}$, define $\widehat\Psi(s):=\int_0^\infty \Psi(y)\,y^{s-1}\,dy$. Then $\widehat\Psi$ is holomorphic for $\mathrm{Re}(s)>0$, extends meromorphically with simple pole at $s=0$ of residue $1$, and obeys $|\widehat\Psi(c+it)|\ll_{c,\Psi,A}(1+|t|)^{-A}$ for every $A>0$ on each vertical strip $\mathrm{Re}(s)\in[c_0,c_1]\subset(0,\infty)$ (use $\Psi\in C^\infty$ so $\widehat\Psi$ is rapidly decreasing).
2. **Theorem 7 (Mellin integral representation).** For any $c_1,\dots,c_4>1/2$,
   $$
   \mathcal{B}(N)\;\le\;\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) \;:=\;\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^4}\int_{(c_1)}\!\!\!\!\cdots\!\!\int_{(c_4)} N^{\sum_i s_i}\Bigl[\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)\Bigr]\mathcal{D}_F^{\ast}(\vec s)\,\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)\,d^4 s.
   $$
3. **Lemma 12 (Local factors — explicit).**
   - $L_p(\vec s)\;=\;1 + \sum_{S\subseteq\{1,2,3,4\},\,|S|\ge 2} 2^{|S|-1}\,\prod_{i\in S} p^{-s_i}$ (one factor per $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$).
   - $M_p(\vec s)\;=\;1 + \sum_{i=1}^4 p^{-s_i}$ (one factor per $p\mid N^2+1$).
4. **Lemma 13 (Closed form for $L_p$).**
   $$
   L_p(\vec s)\;=\;\tfrac12\Bigl[1+\prod_{i=1}^4(1+2p^{-s_i})\Bigr]-\sum_{i=1}^4 p^{-s_i}.
   $$
5. **Theorem 8 (Pair-leading-order extraction).** Set $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w):=\prod_{p\equiv 1(4)}(1-p^{-w})^{-1}$. There is an Euler product $H(\vec s)=\prod_{p\equiv 1(4)} H_p(\vec s)$, absolutely convergent and holomorphic in $\{\vec s:\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>1/3\}$, with $H_p(\vec s)=1+O(p^{-3\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)})$ uniformly in $p$, such that
   $$
   \mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s)\;=\;\Bigl[\prod_{1\le i<j\le 4}\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_i+s_j)^{2}\Bigr]\cdot H(\vec s).
   $$

These five outputs are the decoupled-over-primes representation. Chunk b.3 will shift the four contours and analyze the joint pole/singularity structure at the critical hyperplanes $\{s_i+s_j=1\}_{i<j}$.

## 1. Notation recap

From P16.1.b.1: $\mathcal{F}$ is the set of ordered $4$-tuples $(F_i)_{i=1}^4\in\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 1}^4$ with each $F_i$ squarefree, all primes of $\prod_i F_i$ congruent to $1\pmod 4$, and every such prime dividing $\ge 2$ of the $F_i$'s; $\mathcal{F}^{\ast}=\mathcal{F}\setminus\{(1,1,1,1)\}$. The weight is $W(F)=\prod_{p\in\bigcup_i\mathrm{supp}(F_i)} 2^{|S(p)|-1}$ where $S(p):=\{i:p\mid F_i\}$, with $|S(p)|\ge 2$. The rigid count is

$$
\tilde\Phi(N;F)=\#\{(d_i)_{i=1}^4: d_i\mid N^2+1,\ (d_i)\text{ pwc squarefree},\ d_i\le N/F_i\}.
$$

(Squarefreeness of $d_i$ comes from P16.1.a §1; pairwise coprimality is the unique-prime decomposition.)

We re-encode the box constraint $d_i F_i\le N$ as the simultaneous condition $\prod_i \mathbf 1[d_iF_i\le N]=1$. Theorem 6 of P16.1.b.1 gives

$$
\mathcal{B}(N)\;=\;\sum_{(F_i)\in\mathcal{F}^{\ast}}\;W(F)\;\sum_{(d_i)\text{ pwc sqf div of }N^2+1}\;\prod_{i=1}^4\mathbf 1[d_i F_i\le N].\tag{$\star$}
$$

Write $R(N):=\mathrm{rad}(N^2+1)=\prod_{p\mid N^2+1}p$. Note $d_i\mid R(N)$ throughout, since $d_i$ is squarefree.

## 2. Mellin smoothing (Proposition 11)

Fix once for all $\Psi\in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}_{>0},[0,1])$ with $\Psi\equiv 1$ on $(0,1]$, $\Psi\equiv 0$ on $[2,\infty)$, monotone non-increasing on $[1,2]$. Then $\mathbf 1[y\le 1]\le \Psi(y)$ for all $y>0$.

Its Mellin transform $\widehat\Psi(s):=\int_0^\infty \Psi(y)y^{s-1}dy$ converges absolutely for $\mathrm{Re}(s)>0$ (the singularity at $y=0$ is harmless because $\Psi\equiv 1$ near $0$, contributing $\int_0^1 y^{s-1}dy=1/s$ near the integration boundary $y\to 0$). Splitting the integral at $y=1$ and integrating by parts inside $[1,2]$:

$$
\widehat\Psi(s)\;=\;\frac{1}{s}\;+\;\int_1^2 \Psi(y)y^{s-1}dy\;=\;\frac{1}{s}+E(s),
$$

with $E(s)$ entire. Iterating integration by parts $A$ times on the $[1,2]$ integral (using $\Psi^{(k)}\in C_c^\infty([1,2])$ for $k\ge 1$) gives $|E(s)|\ll_{A,\Psi}(1+|s|)^{-A}$ on any vertical strip; hence

$$
|\widehat\Psi(c+it)|\;\ll_{c,\Psi,A}\;(1+|t|)^{-A}\quad\text{for every }A\ge 1,\ t\in\mathbb R,\ c\in(0,\infty).
$$

By Mellin inversion for $c>0$ and $y>0$, $\Psi(y)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{(c)}\widehat\Psi(s)\,y^{-s}\,ds$. (Outer convergence follows from the rapid-decay bound.)

## 3. Mellin integral representation (Theorem 7)

**Step 1: replace indicator by smooth majorant.** For each $i$, $\mathbf 1[d_iF_i\le N]\le \Psi(d_iF_i/N)$ pointwise (by the defining property of $\Psi$). Multiplying the four inequalities (all factors non-negative) gives $\prod_i\mathbf 1[d_iF_i\le N]\le \prod_i\Psi(d_iF_i/N)$. Substituting into $(\star)$:

$$
\mathcal{B}(N)\;\le\;\sum_{(F_i)\in\mathcal{F}^{\ast}}W(F)\sum_{(d_i)}\prod_i\Psi(d_iF_i/N).
$$

(Note: $\sum_{(F_i)\in\mathcal{F}^{\ast}}$ is independent of $\sum_{(d_i)\text{ pwc sqf div of }N^2+1}$ — there is no support-disjointness constraint coupling the two sums, since constraint (iii) was dropped in Theorem 6 of P16.1.b.1 at a cost absorbed into the $N^{o(1)}$ slack of chunk b.4.)

**Step 2: Mellin-invert each smooth majorant.** By Proposition 11, for any $c_i>0$,

$$
\Psi(d_iF_i/N)\;=\;\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{(c_i)}\widehat\Psi(s_i)\,(N/d_iF_i)^{s_i}\,ds_i.
$$

Substitute and interchange the four contour integrals with the $(F)$- and $(d)$-sums. For $c_i>1/2$ ($i=1,\dots,4$) **strict** absolute convergence is justified by:

- Geometric bound $|p^{-s_i}|\le p^{-1/2}$ for each prime $p$ in the $F$-sum; hence $\sum_{(F_i)\in\mathcal{F}}W(F)\prod_i F_i^{-s_i}$ converges absolutely (cf. §4 below).
- Trivially, $\sum_{(d_i)\text{ pwc sqf div of }N^2+1}\prod_i d_i^{-s_i}\le \prod_{p\mid N^2+1}(1+4p^{-c})\le 5^{\omega(N^2+1)}<\infty$.
- Rapid decay of $\widehat\Psi(c_i+it_i)$ in each $|t_i|$.

After interchange:

$$
\mathcal{B}(N)\;\le\;\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^4}\int_{(c_1)}\!\!\cdots\!\!\int_{(c_4)} N^{\sum_i s_i}\Bigl[\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)\Bigr]\,\Bigl[\sum_{(F_i)\in\mathcal{F}^{\ast}}\!\!W(F)\prod_i F_i^{-s_i}\Bigr]\Bigl[\sum_{(d_i)\text{ pwc}}\prod_i d_i^{-s_i}\Bigr]d^4 s.
$$

Define

$$
\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s)\;:=\;\sum_{(F_i)\in\mathcal{F}}W(F)\prod_i F_i^{-s_i},\qquad \mathcal{D}_F^{\ast}(\vec s)\;:=\;\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s)-1,
$$

$$
\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)\;:=\;\sum_{(d_i)\text{ pwc sqf div of }N^2+1}\prod_i d_i^{-s_i}.
$$

Then

$$
\boxed{\;\mathcal{B}(N)\;\le\;\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N)\;=\;\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^4}\int_{(c)^4} N^{\sum_i s_i}\,\Bigl[\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)\Bigr]\,\mathcal{D}_F^{\ast}(\vec s)\,\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)\,d^4 s\;}\tag{$\dagger$}
$$

for any $c=(c_1,\dots,c_4)$ with each $c_i>1/2$.

## 4. Local factors: Euler products (Lemma 12)

The sums $\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}$ and $\mathcal{D}_d$ both factor as Euler products because each prime appears in at most one "slot" (since each $F_i$ is squarefree, and the $d_i$ are pwc squarefree).

### 4.1. $\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s)=\prod_{p\equiv 1(4)} L_p(\vec s)$

For each prime $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$, the prime $p$ can be assigned to one of:
- $S=\emptyset$: $p$ divides no $F_i$. Local contribution $1$.
- $S\subseteq\{1,2,3,4\}$ with $|S|\ge 2$: $p$ divides $F_i$ for exactly $i\in S$. Local weight $2^{|S|-1}$ (from $W(F)$ multiplicativity), local frequency $\prod_{i\in S}p^{-s_i}$.

Size-$1$ assignments are forbidden by the $\mathcal F$-constraint $|S(p)|\in\{0\}\cup\{2,3,4\}$. Hence

$$
L_p(\vec s)\;=\;1+\sum_{S\subseteq\{1,2,3,4\},\,|S|\ge 2}\;2^{|S|-1}\;\prod_{i\in S}p^{-s_i}.
$$

Cross-prime independence (each prime's slot-choice is independent of others) gives $\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s)=\prod_{p\equiv 1(4)}L_p(\vec s)$ formally; absolute convergence for $\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>1/2$ is verified by $|L_p-1|\le \sum_{|S|\ge 2}2^{|S|-1}p^{-2\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)}\ll p^{-2\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)}$.

### 4.2. $\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)=\prod_{p\mid N^2+1}M_p(\vec s)$

For each prime $p\mid N^2+1$, the prime can be assigned to:
- $S=\emptyset$: $p$ divides no $d_i$. Local $1$.
- $S=\{i\}$ for some $i\in\{1,2,3,4\}$: $p$ divides $d_i$ (and only $d_i$, by pwc). Local $p^{-s_i}$.

Larger $|S|$ excluded by pairwise coprimality of the $d_i$. Hence

$$
M_p(\vec s)\;=\;1+\sum_{i=1}^4 p^{-s_i},
$$

and $\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)=\prod_{p\mid N^2+1}M_p(\vec s)$. This product is finite (only $\omega(N^2+1)$ factors) so converges trivially for all $\vec s$.

This proves Lemma 12. ✓

## 5. Closed form for $L_p$ (Lemma 13)

For variables $u_i:=p^{-s_i}$ we wish to show

$$
1+\sum_{S\subseteq\{1,2,3,4\},|S|\ge 2}2^{|S|-1}\prod_{i\in S}u_i\;=\;\tfrac12\Bigl[1+\prod_{i=1}^4(1+2u_i)\Bigr]-\sum_{i=1}^4 u_i.
$$

Expand $\prod_{i=1}^4(1+2u_i)=\sum_{S\subseteq\{1,2,3,4\}}2^{|S|}\prod_{i\in S}u_i$. Therefore

$$
\tfrac12\Bigl[1+\prod_i(1+2u_i)\Bigr]\;=\;\tfrac12+\tfrac12\sum_S 2^{|S|}\prod_{i\in S}u_i\;=\;\tfrac12+\sum_S 2^{|S|-1}\prod_{i\in S}u_i.
$$

Split $S$ by cardinality: $|S|=0$ contributes $\tfrac12$; $|S|=1$ contributes $\sum_i u_i$; $|S|\ge 2$ contributes the desired sum. So

$$
\tfrac12+\sum_S 2^{|S|-1}\prod_{i\in S}u_i\;=\;\tfrac12+\tfrac12+\sum_i u_i+\sum_{|S|\ge 2}2^{|S|-1}\prod_{i\in S}u_i\;=\;1+\sum_i u_i+(\text{LHS}-1).
$$

Subtracting $\sum_i u_i$ recovers LHS. ✓

**Sanity at small cases.** $u_1=u_2=u$, $u_3=u_4=0$: LHS $=1+2u^2$. RHS $=\tfrac12[1+(1+2u)^2]-2u=\tfrac12[2+4u+4u^2]-2u=1+2u+2u^2-2u=1+2u^2$. ✓ All $u_i=u$: LHS $=1+12u^2+16u^3+8u^4$; RHS $=\tfrac12[1+(1+2u)^4]-4u=\tfrac12[2+8u+24u^2+32u^3+16u^4]-4u=1+12u^2+16u^3+8u^4$. ✓

This closed form will be useful in chunk b.3 because the product $\prod_i(1+2p^{-s_i})$ "almost decouples" $L_p$ into one-variable factors — modulo the additive $-\sum_i p^{-s_i}$ correction.

## 6. Pair-leading-order extraction (Theorem 8)

Define $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w):=\prod_{p\equiv 1\pmod 4}(1-p^{-w})^{-1}$, the partial Euler product over primes $\equiv 1\pmod 4$ (a single-valued Dirichlet series — not a true $L$-function, since it is missing the prime $p=2$ and the primes $\equiv 3\pmod 4$). The standard factorization

$$
\zeta(w)\,L(w,\chi_{-4})\;=\;(1-2^{-w})^{-1}\cdot\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2\cdot\!\!\prod_{p\equiv 3\pmod 4}\!\!(1-p^{-2w})^{-1}
$$

(from $\chi_{-4}(2)=0$, $\chi_{-4}(p)=+1$ on $p\equiv 1\pmod 4$, $\chi_{-4}(p)=-1$ on $p\equiv 3\pmod 4$) exhibits $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2$ as a meromorphic continuation: $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2$ has a simple pole at $w=1$ inherited from $\zeta(w)$ (since $L(1,\chi_{-4})\ne 0$, $1-2^{-w}\ne 0$, and the auxiliary product over $p\equiv 3\pmod 4$ converges absolutely for $\mathrm{Re}(w)>1/2$). Classical zero-free regions for $\zeta$ and $L(\cdot,\chi_{-4})$ guarantee $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2$ is holomorphic and non-vanishing in a region $\mathrm{Re}(w)\ge 1-c/\log(2+|\mathrm{Im}\,w|)$, $w\ne 1$. For chunk b.3 we will only need (i) the simple pole at $w=1$, (ii) polynomial-growth bounds in $|\mathrm{Im}\,w|$ on vertical strips $\mathrm{Re}(w)\in[1-c/\log,1+\varepsilon]$, both standard.

**Claim.** For $\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>1/3$ the Euler product

$$
H(\vec s)\;:=\;\prod_{p\equiv 1\pmod 4}H_p(\vec s),\qquad H_p(\vec s)\;:=\;\frac{L_p(\vec s)}{\prod_{1\le i<j\le 4}(1-p^{-s_i-s_j})^{-2}}
$$

converges absolutely and is holomorphic, with $H_p(\vec s)=1+O(p^{-3\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)})$ uniformly in $p$.

*Proof.* The key point is that the conclusion only requires matching the degree-$\le 2$ terms of $L_p$ and the denominator; degree-$3$ and higher are not needed.

Define the "degree" of a monomial $\prod_i p^{-k_i s_i}$ (with $k_i \in \mathbb Z_{\ge 0}$) as $\sum_i k_i$. Each $|p^{-k_i s_i}|=p^{-k_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)}$, so any monomial of degree $\ge d$ is bounded by $p^{-d\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)}$.

**Degree $\le 2$ terms agree exactly.**

- $L_p$ degree $\le 2$: from Lemma 12, $L_p = 1 + 2\sum_{i<j}p^{-s_i-s_j} + (\text{terms of degree}\ge 3)$, where the degree-$\ge 3$ contributions are $4\sum_{|S|=3}\prod_{k\in S}p^{-s_k}$ (degree $3$) and $8 p^{-s_1-s_2-s_3-s_4}$ (degree $4$).
- Denominator degree $\le 2$: $\prod_{i<j}(1-p^{-s_i-s_j})^{-2}=\prod_{i<j}\bigl[1+2p^{-s_i-s_j}+(\text{degree}\ge 4)\bigr]$. Each individual factor's contribution from "$(1-p^{-s_i-s_j})^{-2}-1-2p^{-s_i-s_j}$" is $3p^{-2(s_i+s_j)}+\cdots$, of degree $\ge 4$. Cross-terms between two factors multiply two of the degree-$\ge 2$ pieces and so contribute degree $\ge 4$. Therefore the degree-$\le 2$ part of the denominator is exactly $1 + 2\sum_{i<j}p^{-s_i-s_j}$.

Hence the degree-$\le 2$ parts of $L_p$ and the denominator agree exactly.

**Conclusion.** Write $L_p = 1 + B$, denominator $= 1+ A$, with $A, B = O(p^{-2\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)})$ both holomorphic functions of $\vec s$ (entire, in fact polynomial in $p^{-s_i}$ in the case of $L_p$, and absolutely convergent power series in $p^{-s_i}$ in the case of the denominator, in $\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>0$). By the analysis above, $B - A$ has zero degree-$\le 2$ part, so $B - A = O(p^{-3\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)})$ uniformly in $\vec s$ (with implicit constant depending only on $4$, the number of variables). The leading degree-$3$ contribution to $B-A$ is $4\sum_{|S|=3}\prod_{k\in S}p^{-s_k}$ (present in $L_p$, absent in the denominator).

Then $H_p - 1 = (1+B)/(1+A) - 1 = (B-A)/(1+A)$. For $\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)$ large enough that $|A|\le 1/2$ (e.g., $\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)\ge 1/3$ and $p\ge p_0$ a uniform threshold), $|1+A|\ge 1/2$, hence $|H_p - 1|\le 2|B-A|=O(p^{-3\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)})$. For the finitely many $p<p_0$ the local factor is bounded by the standard analysis (only finitely many "bad" primes contribute a bounded multiplicative constant to $H$).

The Euler product $H(\vec s)=\prod_p H_p(\vec s)=\prod_p\bigl(1+(H_p-1)\bigr)$ converges absolutely iff $\sum_p|H_p-1|<\infty$, i.e., $\sum_p p^{-3\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)}<\infty$, i.e., $\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>1/3$. ✓

This proves Theorem 8: $\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s)=\bigl[\prod_{i<j}\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_i+s_j)^2\bigr]\cdot H(\vec s)$, with $H$ holomorphic past the critical hyperplanes $\{s_i+s_j=1\}$ down to $\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>1/3$. ✓

## 7. Final decoupled formula (consolidation)

Substituting into $(\dagger)$:

$$
\mathcal{B}(N)\;\le\;\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^4}\int_{(c)^4} N^{\sum_i s_i}\,\Bigl[\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)\Bigr]\,\Bigl[\prod_{i<j}\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_i+s_j)^2\cdot H(\vec s)-1\Bigr]\,\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)\,d^4 s. \tag{$\ddagger$}
$$

This is the decoupled-over-primes representation. The integrand has:
- An $N^{\sum_i s_i}$ size factor (controls $N$-dependence).
- A smoothing factor $\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)$, holomorphic for $\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>0$ (simple pole at each $s_i=0$ of residue $1$), rapidly decaying on each vertical line.
- A divisor factor $\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)$ — finite Euler product over primes of $N^2+1$, bounded by $\prod_p(1+4p^{-c})\le \prod_p 5=5^{\omega(N^2+1)}$.
- A spin factor $\prod_{i<j}\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_i+s_j)^2\cdot H(\vec s)-1$ — six $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}$-type poles on the hyperplanes $s_i+s_j=1$.

**Crucial features.** The factor $\prod_{i<j}\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_i+s_j)^2$ has its "joint critical point" at $s_1=s_2=s_3=s_4=1/2$, where all six hyperplanes $s_i+s_j=1$ meet. The size of $N^{\sum s_i}$ at this point is $N^2$. This matches the target $\mathcal{B}(N)\le K^2(\log N)^B$ in the $K$-uniform $N$-scaling sense (since $K^2$ has no explicit $N$-power but $\tilde\Phi(N;F)$ contributes a $\tau$-type bound).

The $K^2$ factor of the final target must come from $\mathcal{D}_d$ — see §8.

## 8. Setup for chunk P16.1.b.3

The goal of b.3 is to deform contours and bound $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N)$ by $C\cdot K(N)^2(\log N)^B$ for an explicit $B$.

**Strategy outline.** Shift all four contours from $\mathrm{Re}(s_i)=c$ ($c>1/2$) to $\mathrm{Re}(s_i)=\delta$ ($0<\delta<1/2$) — i.e., move the contours through the critical hyperplanes $\{s_i+s_j=1\}$. Each contour crossing picks up residues of $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_i+s_j)^2$ along the corresponding hyperplane, contributing $\log N$ factors and dropping the $N^{\sum s_i}$ power to a smaller value.

Concretely, after all six crossings the integrand sits in a region where:
- $\mathrm{Re}(s_i)<1/2$ for all $i$, so $N^{\sum s_i}<N^2$.
- All $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}$ are away from the critical hyperplane $s_i+s_j=1$ (so bounded).
- The leading $K^2$ contribution comes from the residual integral, where $\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)$ contributes a factor bounded in terms of $K(N)$ via $|\mathcal{D}_d(\delta+it)|\le \prod_p(1+4 p^{-\delta})\le \exp(4\sum_{p\mid N^2+1}p^{-\delta})$.

For $\delta$ near $1/2$, $\sum_{p\mid N^2+1}p^{-\delta}\le \omega(N^2+1)\cdot p_{\min}^{-\delta}$ but a sharper $L^2$-type bound (cf. Erdős–Pomerance or Hooley $\Delta$-function) gives effective $K^c(\log K)^?$ control.

**Three sub-chunks for b.3.**
- **b.3.A:** Compute the residue at the hyperplane $s_4=1-s_3$ (an instance of the simple pole of $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_3+s_4)^2$ at $s_3+s_4=1$). Verify the resulting 3-variable integrand.
- **b.3.B:** Iterate the residue extraction for the remaining five hyperplanes. Track the cumulative $\log N$ exponents.
- **b.3.C:** Bound $\mathcal{D}_d$ on the final $\delta$-shifted contour; conclude $\mathcal{B}(N)\le C\cdot K(N)^{\alpha}(\log N)^B$ with explicit $\alpha,B$. Optimistic target: $\alpha=2$, $B\le 8$.

**Alternative route (b.3').** If the residue iteration is unwieldy, fall back to a direct $\tau_k$-moment bound on $\mathcal{B}(N)$ via dyadic decomposition (Approach B of P16.1.b.1 §6) — applied to the $F$-sum after we've already isolated $\mathcal{D}_F^*$.
