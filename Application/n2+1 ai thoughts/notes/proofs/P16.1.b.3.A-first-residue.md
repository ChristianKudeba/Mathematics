# P16.1.b.3.A. First-hyperplane residue of $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N)$

## Status

First of three sub-chunks of chunk **P16.1.b.3** (continuation of `P16.1.b.2-mellin-decoupling.md`). The deliverable is the explicit residue formula for the $s_4$-contour shift past the cluster of three hyperplanes $\{s_4 = 1-s_k\}_{k=1,2,3}$ that all lie at real part $1-c$, together with the contour-shift identity

$$
\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) \;=\; \mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c,c,c,\delta)}(N) \;+\; 3\,R^{(4)}(N),
$$

where $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c,c,c,\delta)}(N)$ is the original 4-fold integral with the $s_4$-contour shifted to $\mathrm{Re}(s_4)=\delta\in(1/3,1-c)$, and $R^{(4)}(N)$ is the 3-fold residual integral computed below.

**Corrections to the prior-session pickup hint.** Two small mis-statements in the b.3 setup of `P16.1.b.2-mellin-decoupling.md` §8 are fixed in this file:

1. **No $\log N$ from a single residue.** $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2$ has a *simple* pole at $w=1$ (because $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)$ itself has a branch point of order $1/2$ at $w=1$; squaring promotes this to a simple pole, not a double pole). Hence the residue at $s_4=1-s_3$ contributes only an $N^1$ factor, not an $N\log N$ factor. Logarithmic powers will emerge from *coalescences* during iteration (chunk b.3.B), where two residues hit the same hyperplane simultaneously, not from a single contour shift.
2. **Three residues, not one, per shift.** When $\mathrm{Re}(s_1)=\mathrm{Re}(s_2)=\mathrm{Re}(s_3)=c$, the three poles of the integrand in $s_4$ at $s_4=1-s_1,\,1-s_2,\,1-s_3$ all sit at the same real part $1-c$, distinct generic imaginary parts. A single $s_4$-contour shift catches all three. By $S_3$-symmetry of the integrand in the variables $\{s_1,s_2,s_3\}$, the three residue integrals are equal, giving the factor $3$ in the identity above.

## 1. Notation recap

From `P16.1.b.2-mellin-decoupling.md`: for any $c=(c_1,\dots,c_4)$ with each $c_i>1/2$,

$$
\mathcal{B}(N) \;\le\; \mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) \;=\; \frac{1}{(2\pi i)^4}\int_{(c_1)}\!\!\!\cdots\!\!\int_{(c_4)} I(\vec s)\,d^4 s,\quad
I(\vec s) := N^{\sum_i s_i}\Bigl[\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)\Bigr]\mathcal{D}_F^{\ast}(\vec s)\,\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s),
$$

with $\mathcal{D}_F^{\ast}=\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}-1$ and the explicit factorization (Theorem 8 of b.2)

$$
\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s) \;=\; \Bigl[\prod_{1\le i<j\le 4}\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_i+s_j)^{2}\Bigr]\cdot H(\vec s),
$$

valid in $\min_i\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>1/3$, where $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w):=\prod_{p\equiv 1(4)}(1-p^{-w})^{-1}$ and $H$ is holomorphic and absolutely convergent in that half-plane.

We work with $c_1=c_2=c_3=c_4=c=1/2+\epsilon_0$ for a small fixed $\epsilon_0\in(0,1/6)$, and we will shift the $s_4$-contour to $\mathrm{Re}(s_4)=\delta$ with $1/3<\delta<1-c=1/2-\epsilon_0$. The constraint $\epsilon_0<1/6$ is precisely what makes the interval $(1/3, 1/2-\epsilon_0)$ non-empty; we keep it explicit throughout.

## 2. The residue at a simple pole of $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$

**Lemma 14 (Simple-pole structure of $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$).** Define $\kappa := \mathrm{Res}_{w=1}\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2$. Then

$$
\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2 \;=\; \frac{\kappa}{w-1} + \mathcal{R}(w),
$$

where $\mathcal{R}(w)$ is holomorphic in a neighborhood of $w=1$ (in fact in the standard zero-free region of $\zeta\cdot L(\,\cdot,\chi_{-4})$, $w\ne 1$). Moreover

$$
\kappa \;=\; \frac{L(1,\chi_{-4})}{2\,\zeta^{(3,4)}(2)} \;=\; \frac{\pi/4}{2\,\zeta^{(3,4)}(2)} \;>\;0,
$$

with $\zeta^{(3,4)}(s):=\prod_{p\equiv 3(4)}(1-p^{-s})^{-1}$ (a finite positive constant at $s=2$).

*Proof.* From the standard factorization (b.2 §6)

$$
\zeta(w)\,L(w,\chi_{-4}) \;=\; (1-2^{-w})^{-1}\,\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2\,\prod_{p\equiv 3(4)}(1-p^{-2w})^{-1},
$$

solving for $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2$ and using that the right-hand-side factors $(1-2^{-w})$ and $\prod_{p\equiv 3(4)}(1-p^{-2w})^{-1}$ are holomorphic and non-vanishing in $\mathrm{Re}(w)>1/2$:

$$
\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2 \;=\; (1-2^{-w})\cdot\frac{\zeta(w)\,L(w,\chi_{-4})}{\prod_{p\equiv 3(4)}(1-p^{-2w})^{-1}}.
$$

$\zeta(w)$ has a simple pole at $w=1$ with residue $1$; all other factors are holomorphic and non-vanishing at $w=1$ ($L(1,\chi_{-4})=\pi/4\ne 0$). Hence $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2$ has a simple pole at $w=1$ with residue

$$
\kappa \;=\; (1-2^{-1})\cdot\frac{L(1,\chi_{-4})}{\zeta^{(3,4)}(2)} \;=\; \tfrac12\cdot\frac{\pi/4}{\zeta^{(3,4)}(2)}.\qquad\Box
$$

(*Remark.* $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)$ itself is *not* meromorphic at $w=1$: $\log\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)=\sum_{p\equiv 1(4)}p^{-w}+O(1)\sim\tfrac12\log\tfrac1{w-1}$ near $w=1$ (Dirichlet density), so $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)\sim C(w-1)^{-1/2}$ has a branch singularity. Squaring promotes the branch to the simple pole above.)

**Lemma 14 ′ (Domain of meromorphic extension of $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$).** Rearranging the factorization of Lemma 14,

$$
\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2 \;=\; (1-2^{-w})\,\Bigl[\prod_{p\equiv 3(4)}(1-p^{-2w})\Bigr]\,\zeta(w)\,L(w,\chi_{-4}).
$$

On $\mathrm{Re}(w)>1/2$, the auxiliary factors $(1-2^{-w})$ and $\prod_{p\equiv 3(4)}(1-p^{-2w})$ are absolutely convergent and holomorphic (the latter because $\sum_{p\equiv 3(4)}p^{-2\mathrm{Re}(w)}<\infty$ for $\mathrm{Re}(w)>1/2$); their possible zeros lie outside any vertical strip avoiding $w\in\{2\pi i k/\log 2\}_k$ (for $1-2^{-w}$) and outside our region of interest. Therefore

> **$\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2$ is meromorphic on the half-plane $\mathrm{Re}(w)>1/2$, with a single simple pole at $w=1$ and no other poles. Zeros of $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2$ in $\mathrm{Re}(w)>1/2$ coincide with zeros of $\zeta(w)L(w,\chi_{-4})$ in that region (i.e., non-trivial zeros of $\zeta$ and $L$ inside the critical strip's upper half $1/2<\mathrm{Re}(w)<1$).**

In particular: in the strip we use during the contour shift, $\mathrm{Re}(s_k+s_4)\in(c+\delta,c+c)\subset(5/6,1+2\epsilon_0)$, there are **no additional poles** of $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_k+s_4)^2$ beyond the simple pole at $s_k+s_4=1$ enumerated in §3 below. (Non-trivial zeros of $\zeta L$ in this strip would be ZEROS of $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$, hence not crossings of the contour shift.)

## 3. The pole structure of $I(\vec s)$ in $s_4$

Fix $(s_1,s_2,s_3)$ with $\mathrm{Re}(s_k)=c=1/2+\epsilon_0$ for $k=1,2,3$. View $I(\vec s)$ as a function of $s_4$ in the strip $\delta\le\mathrm{Re}(s_4)\le c$.

**Singularities in $s_4$ inside the strip.**

- *From $\widehat\Psi(s_4)$:* simple pole at $s_4=0$. Since $\delta>1/3>0$, this is **outside the strip** $[\delta,c]\times i\mathbb R$. Not crossed.
- *From $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_k+s_4)^2$ for $k=1,2,3$:* simple poles at $s_4=1-s_k$ (Lemma 14). The real part of each is $1-c=1/2-\epsilon_0\in(\delta,c)$. **All three crossed** by the contour shift.
- *From $\mathcal{D}_d$ at $s_4$:* the local factor $M_p(\vec s)=1+\sum_i p^{-s_i}$ never vanishes for $\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>0$ (since $|p^{-s_i}|<1$ and the sum is $\ge 1-4 p^{-\mathrm{Re}(s_i)}\ge 1-4 p^{-\delta}>0$ for $p$ above a finite uniform threshold). For the finitely many "small primes" $p\mid N^2+1$ with $p\le p_0$, the local factor remains positive (it is a real positive number when $\vec s$ is real). Holomorphic in $s_4$ throughout the strip.
- *From $H(\vec s)$:* holomorphic in $\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>1/3\supset[\delta,c]\times i\mathbb R$ (in $s_4$, with the other three variables fixed at $\mathrm{Re}=c$).
- *From the $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_i+s_j)^2$ factors with $(i,j)$ not involving $s_4$:* the three factors $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^2$, $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_3)^2$, $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+s_3)^2$ are constants in $s_4$. They are holomorphic in $s_4$.
- *From the constant "$-1$" subtraction $\mathcal{D}_F^{\ast}=\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}-1$:* holomorphic in $s_4$.

So only the three poles at $s_4=1-s_k$, $k\in\{1,2,3\}$, lie in the strip.

**Genericity.** For generic $(s_1,s_2,s_3)$ on the contour $(c+i\mathbb R)^3$ (i.e., outside the codimension-1 subvariety where $\mathrm{Im}(s_i)=\mathrm{Im}(s_j)$ for some $i\ne j$), the three poles in $s_4$ are pairwise distinct. The coalescence locus is measure-zero, so the residue formula below is valid almost everywhere on the outer contour, hence under integration.

## 4. Residue formula (Theorem 9)

**Theorem 9 (First-hyperplane residue).** Fix $(s_1,s_2,s_3)$ generic with $\mathrm{Re}(s_k)=c$ for $k=1,2,3$. Then

$$
\mathrm{Res}_{s_4=1-s_k}I(\vec s) \;=\; \kappa\cdot N\cdot N^{\,s_i+s_j+s_l-s_l}\,\widehat\Psi(s_i)\,\widehat\Psi(s_j)\,\widehat\Psi(s_l)\,\widehat\Psi(1-s_l)\cdot G^{(l)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)\cdot \mathcal{D}_d^{(l)}(s_1,s_2,s_3),
$$

where $\{i,j,l\}=\{1,2,3\}$ with $l=k$, and

$$
\begin{aligned}
G^{(l)}(\vec s_{\{1,2,3\}}) \;&:=\; \Bigl[\prod_{\substack{1\le m<n\le 4\\ (m,n)\ne(l,4)}}\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_m+s_n)^2\Bigr]_{s_4=1-s_l}\cdot H(s_1,s_2,s_3,1-s_l)\\
\mathcal{D}_d^{(l)}(\vec s_{\{1,2,3\}}) \;&:=\; \mathcal{D}_d(s_1,s_2,s_3,1-s_l) \;=\; \prod_{p\mid N^2+1}\!\Bigl[1+p^{-s_1}+p^{-s_2}+p^{-s_3}+p^{-(1-s_l)}\Bigr].
\end{aligned}
$$

In particular, taking $l=3$ for concreteness:

$$
\mathrm{Res}_{s_4=1-s_3}I(\vec s) \;=\; \kappa\,N\,N^{s_1+s_2}\,\widehat\Psi(s_1)\widehat\Psi(s_2)\widehat\Psi(s_3)\widehat\Psi(1-s_3)\, G^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)\, \mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3),
$$

with $G^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)= \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^2\,\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_3)^2\,\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+1-s_3)^2\,\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+s_3)^2\,\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+1-s_3)^2\,H(s_1,s_2,s_3,1-s_3)$.

*Proof.* Write $I(\vec s)= N^{\sum_i s_i}\bigl[\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)\bigr] (\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s)-1)\,\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)$. The "$-1$" contribution is holomorphic in $s_4$ throughout the strip (it has no $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$ factor), so

$$
\mathrm{Res}_{s_4=1-s_k}I(\vec s) \;=\; \mathrm{Res}_{s_4=1-s_k}\Bigl[N^{\sum_i s_i}\bigl(\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)\bigr)\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}(\vec s)\,\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)\Bigr].
$$

Factor $\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}=\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_k+s_4)^2\cdot G^{(k)}_{\mathrm{aux}}(\vec s)$ with

$$
G^{(k)}_{\mathrm{aux}}(\vec s) := \Bigl[\prod_{\substack{1\le m<n\le 4\\ (m,n)\ne (k,4)}}\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_m+s_n)^2\Bigr]\cdot H(\vec s).
$$

By Lemma 14, $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_k+s_4)^2 = \kappa/(s_k+s_4-1)+\mathcal{R}(s_k+s_4)$, so it has a simple pole at $s_4=1-s_k$ with residue (in $s_4$) equal to $\kappa$ (the Jacobian $d(s_k+s_4)/ds_4=1$). All other factors of the integrand are holomorphic and non-vanishing at $s_4=1-s_k$: $G^{(k)}_{\mathrm{aux}}$ is holomorphic there because (i) the remaining five $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$ factors do not involve $s_k+s_4$, hence their potential singularities are at $s_4=1-s_m$ for $m\ne k$, which are at a different point for generic configuration; (ii) $H$ is holomorphic in the open half-plane $\min\mathrm{Re}(\cdot)>1/3$ by Theorem 8. And $\widehat\Psi(s_4)\big|_{s_4=1-s_k}=\widehat\Psi(1-s_k)$ is well-defined since $\mathrm{Re}(1-s_k)=1-c\in(0,1/2)$, away from the pole at $s_4=0$. Therefore

$$
\mathrm{Res}_{s_4=1-s_k}I(\vec s) = \kappa\cdot \bigl[\text{integrand without }\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_k+s_4)^2,\text{ evaluated at }s_4=1-s_k\bigr],
$$

which is the stated formula after expanding $N^{\sum_i s_i}\big|_{s_4=1-s_k}= N^{s_1+s_2+s_3+s_4}\big|_{s_4=1-s_k}= N\cdot N^{s_1+s_2+s_3-s_k}= N\cdot N^{s_i+s_j}$ for $\{i,j\}=\{1,2,3\}\setminus\{k\}$.\qquad$\Box$

## 5. Contour-shift identity (Theorem 10)

**Theorem 10 (Single-variable contour shift in $s_4$).** With the above setup, for $1/3<\delta<1-c$,

$$
\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) \;=\; \mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c,c,c,\delta)}(N) \;+\; \sum_{k=1}^{3} R^{(4)}_{k}(N),
$$

where

$$
\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c,c,c,\delta)}(N) \;:=\; \frac{1}{(2\pi i)^4}\int_{(c)^3\times(\delta)} I(\vec s)\,d^4 s
$$

and

$$
R^{(4)}_{k}(N) \;:=\; \frac{1}{(2\pi i)^3}\int_{(c)^3}\mathrm{Res}_{s_4=1-s_k}\!I(\vec s)\,d^3 s.
$$

Moreover, $R^{(4)}_1(N)=R^{(4)}_2(N)=R^{(4)}_3(N)=:R^{(4)}(N)$, so

$$
\boxed{\;\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) \;=\; \mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c,c,c,\delta)}(N) \;+\; 3\,R^{(4)}(N)\;}\tag{$\star\star$}
$$

with

$$
R^{(4)}(N) \;=\; \kappa\,N\,\cdot\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^3}\int_{(c)^3} N^{s_1+s_2}\,\widehat\Psi(s_1)\widehat\Psi(s_2)\widehat\Psi(s_3)\widehat\Psi(1-s_3)\, G^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)\,\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)\,d^3 s.
$$

*Proof of single-variable shift.* For fixed $(s_1,s_2,s_3)\in(c+i\mathbb R)^3$ generic (off the coalescence locus), apply Cauchy's residue theorem on a long rectangle $\delta\le\mathrm{Re}(s_4)\le c$, $|\mathrm{Im}(s_4)|\le T$, and let $T\to\infty$. The top and bottom edges contribute $0$ in the limit by the rapid decay $|\widehat\Psi(s_4)|\ll(1+|\mathrm{Im}(s_4)|)^{-A}$ (Proposition 11 of b.2), uniformly in $\mathrm{Re}(s_4)\in[\delta,c]$, against the at-most-polynomial growth of all other factors in $|\mathrm{Im}(s_4)|$.

(Polynomial-growth justification: $\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)$ is a finite product of degree at most $4\omega(N^2+1)$ in $\{p^{-s_i}\}$, hence bounded in $|\mathrm{Im}(s_4)|$ on the strip $[\delta,c]\times i\mathbb R$. $H(\vec s)$ is absolutely convergent and uniformly bounded on $\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>1/3$. For each $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_k+s_4)^2$, the factorization $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2 = (1-2^{-w})\prod_{p\equiv 3(4)}(1-p^{-2w})\,\zeta(w)L(w,\chi_{-4})$ combined with classical convexity bounds $|\zeta(\sigma+it)|\ll(1+|t|)^{(1-\sigma)/2+\varepsilon}$ for $0\le\sigma\le 1$ and similarly for $L(\cdot,\chi_{-4})$ — see Iwaniec–Kowalski Thm 5.23 — gives $|\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(\sigma+it)^2|\ll(1+|t|)^{(1-\sigma)+\varepsilon}$ on vertical lines $\sigma\in(1/2,1)$, polynomial growth as required. The constant-in-$s_4$ factors $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_i+s_j)^2$ for $i,j\in\{1,2,3\}$ contribute fixed multiplicative constants.)

The residue theorem then identifies the difference of the integrals over $(c)$ and $(\delta)$ with the sum of the three residues inside the strip.

The result holds for almost every outer $(s_1,s_2,s_3)$. Integrating over the outer contour using Fubini (justified by absolute convergence of the original 4-fold integral, plus absolute convergence on the shifted contour with $\delta>1/3$):

$$
\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^4}\int_{(c)^4} I\,d^4 s \;=\; \frac{1}{(2\pi i)^4}\int_{(c)^3\times(\delta)} I\,d^4 s \;+\; \sum_{k=1}^3 \frac{1}{(2\pi i)^3}\int_{(c)^3} \mathrm{Res}_{s_4=1-s_k}I\,d^3 s.
$$

*Symmetry argument $R^{(4)}_1=R^{(4)}_2=R^{(4)}_3$.* The integrand $I(\vec s) = N^{\sum_i s_i}\bigl[\prod_i \widehat\Psi(s_i)\bigr]\mathcal{D}_F^{\ast}(\vec s)\,\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)$ is **$S_4$-symmetric**: each factor depends on $\{s_1,s_2,s_3,s_4\}$ symmetrically. ($N^{\sum_i s_i}$, $\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)$ are obviously symmetric; $\mathcal{D}_F^{\mathcal{F}}=\prod_p L_p$ with $L_p$ symmetric in $(s_i)$ per Lemma 12 of b.2; $\mathcal{D}_d=\prod_p M_p$ with $M_p$ symmetric per same.) Hence $I$ is invariant under any permutation of $(s_1,s_2,s_3,s_4)$.

Consider the transposition $\tau_{13}:\{1,2,3\}\to\{1,2,3\}$ swapping indices $1\leftrightarrow 3$ on the integration variables only (with $s_4$ held as the residue variable). Under the relabeling $(s_1,s_2,s_3)\mapsto(s_3',s_2',s_1')$ in the integration variables (rename then drop primes), the integration domain $(c+i\mathbb R)^3$ is invariant; the integrand $I(\vec s)$ is invariant (by $S_4$-symmetry of $I$, in particular under $S_3$ on $\{s_1,s_2,s_3\}$); and the residue condition "$s_4=1-s_1$" becomes "$s_4=1-s_3$". Hence $R^{(4)}_1=R^{(4)}_3$. Similarly $\tau_{23}$ gives $R^{(4)}_2=R^{(4)}_3$. \qquad$\Box$

## 6. Sanity check: $N$-power tally

The $N^{\sum_i s_i}$ factor at the residue point $s_4=1-s_3$ becomes $N^{s_1+s_2+s_3+(1-s_3)}=N^{1+s_1+s_2}$, so the residual integrand carries a factor of $N$ times a $3$-fold integrand of size $N^{s_1+s_2}$ on contours $\mathrm{Re}(s_1)=\mathrm{Re}(s_2)=c=1/2+\epsilon_0$. The total $N$-power scale is therefore $N^{1+c+c}=N^{2+2\epsilon_0}$ at the level of the integrand on the new contour, exceeding $N^2$ by a small amount $N^{2\epsilon_0}$ — the cost of "not having shifted $s_1, s_2$ yet". The remaining $s_1$- and $s_2$-shifts in chunk b.3.B will collapse this back below $N^2$.

**Comparison to target.** The desired bound is $\mathcal{B}(N)\le C\,K(N)^2(\log N)^B$. Since $K(N)=\tau(N^2+1)/4\ll N^{o(1)}$ for typical $N$ and $K(N)\ll N^{1/(\log\log N)+o(1)}$ in worst case, the target is essentially $N^{o(1)}(\log N)^B$. Our residual carries an $N^1$ factor (from the residue) AND an $N^{s_1+s_2}\sim N^{1+2\epsilon_0}$ factor (from the outer integral), totaling $N^{2+2\epsilon_0}$ — too large by $N^2$. **This is expected**: the subsequent residue extractions in b.3.B will provide the cancellation. Specifically, three more contour shifts (one each for $s_1, s_2, s_3$) will each contribute a similar $\sum_{k}N^{1-s_k}\cdot\ldots$ structure, and the eventual residual will be on the "fully shifted" contour $\mathrm{Re}(s_i)=\delta$ for all $i$, giving total $N^{4\delta}<N^2$ on that contour.

## 7. Analytic structure of $G^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)$ — preview for b.3.B

The 3-variable function $G^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)$ inherits the following singularities (relevant for the next iteration):

- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^2$: simple pole at $s_1+s_2=1$.
- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_3)^2$: simple pole at $s_1+s_3=1$.
- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+s_3)^2$: simple pole at $s_2+s_3=1$.
- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+1-s_3)^2$: simple pole at $s_1+1-s_3=1$, i.e., **the new hyperplane $s_1=s_3$**.
- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+1-s_3)^2$: simple pole at **$s_2=s_3$** (new).

**Key observation.** The substitution $s_4\to 1-s_3$ converts the original symmetric pole set $\{s_i+s_j=1\}_{i<j}$ into a *broken* pole set with two NEW pole hyperplanes $s_1=s_3$ and $s_2=s_3$. These "diagonal" poles were absent in the 4-fold integrand and emerge purely from the iteration.

This complicates chunk b.3.B: when we shift the $s_3$-contour next, we will cross not only the original-type hyperplane $\{s_1+s_3=1\}\cup\{s_2+s_3=1\}$ but also the new diagonal hyperplanes $\{s_3=s_1\}\cup\{s_3=s_2\}$. The diagonal poles have a *different* coefficient in front (still proportional to $\kappa$, but inside a different combinatorial structure), and the iteration becomes more intricate than a naïve "six original hyperplanes" count would suggest.

**Plan for b.3.B.** Compute the residue at $s_3=s_1$ (a diagonal pole) and at $s_1+s_3=1$ (an original-type pole), separately; verify they contribute to the same $N$-power tier $N^{1}$ (from a single shift); and then iterate to fully shift all four contours.

## 8. Convergence on the new contour

On the new contour $(c)^3\times(\delta)$ with $\delta\in(1/3,1-c)$, the 4-fold integral $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c,c,c,\delta)}(N)$ converges absolutely:

- $\widehat\Psi(s_i)$ contributes rapid decay in each $|\mathrm{Im}(s_i)|$.
- $H(\vec s)$ is bounded on $\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i)>1/3$ (Theorem 8 of b.2).
- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_m+s_n)^2$ on the new contour: real parts $\mathrm{Re}(s_m+s_n)\in\{c+\delta, 2c, c+\delta\}$ where the smallest is $c+\delta\in(1/3+1/2, 1/2+1/2)=(5/6, 1)$. Inside the strip $\sigma\in(5/6, 1)$ each $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(\sigma+it)^2$ grows at most polynomially in $|t|$ by standard convexity (combined with the rapid decay of $\widehat\Psi$, this gives integrable behavior).
- $\mathcal{D}_d(\vec s)$ is bounded uniformly in $\vec s$ on the new contour by $\prod_{p\mid N^2+1}(1+4 p^{-\delta})\le (5)^{\omega(N^2+1)}$, finite.

For the residual integral $R^{(4)}(N)$ on the 3-fold contour $(c)^3$, convergence is similar (one fewer variable, same arguments).

## 9. Residual integrand size: a first crude bound

Pointwise on the contour $(c+i\mathbb R)^3$, we have $|N^{s_1+s_2}|=N^{2c}=N^{1+2\epsilon_0}$ and $|N^1|=N$ from the residue. To bound $|G^{(3)}|$, use the Hadamard-type bound for $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$ inside the half-plane $\mathrm{Re}(w)\ge \alpha>1/2$:

$$
|\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(\sigma+it)^2|\;\ll_\alpha\;(1+|t|)^{C(\alpha)},
$$

for a constant $C(\alpha)<\infty$ depending only on $\alpha$. Hence on $(c+i\mathbb R)^3$ each of the five $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$-factors in $G^{(3)}$ is of polynomial growth in $|\vec t|$, and $H$ is bounded. Combined with the rapid decay of $\prod_i\widehat\Psi(s_i)$, the integral $R^{(4)}(N)$ converges absolutely, and by very crude $L^\infty$ bound,

$$
|R^{(4)}(N)|\;\ll\; N^{1+2\epsilon_0}\cdot \kappa\cdot|\mathcal{D}_d|_{\infty}\cdot \int_{\mathbb R^3}\prod_i(1+|t_i|)^{-A}\,d^3 t\;\ll\; N^{1+2\epsilon_0}\cdot 5^{\omega(N^2+1)}.
$$
