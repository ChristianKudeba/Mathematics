# P16.1.b.3.B. $s_3$-contour shift of $R^{(4)}(N)$ and emergence of the $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^4$ double pole

## Status

Second of three (or four) sub-chunks of chunk **P16.1.b.3** (continuation of `P16.1.b.3.A-first-residue.md`). The deliverable here is the explicit $s_3$-contour shift of the residual integral $R^{(4)}(N)$ defined in Theorem 10 of b.3.A.

The main new structural fact is that, after lifting the outer $s_1, s_2$ contours by a small amount $\eta > 0$, the only poles in $s_3$ crossed during the shift to the line $\mathrm{Re}(s_3) = \delta$ are the two **original-type** poles $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ and $s_3 = 1 - s_2$. The two **diagonal** poles $s_3 = s_1$, $s_3 = s_2$ are pushed outside the strip on the right by the lift, and so are NOT crossed at this stage — they will resurface only when we further shift $s_1$ or $s_2$.

Computing one of the residues then reveals an emergent **double pole** $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^4$ in the residual integrand, which is precisely the structural feature predicted in b.3.A §1 to produce the first $\log N$ factor at the next iteration. We also identify a new factor $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(2 s_1)^2$ with a simple pole at $s_1 = 1/2$ (inside the upcoming strip), and a new anti-diagonal factor $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - s_1)^2$ with a pole at $s_1 = s_2$ (on the boundary of the strip).

## 1. Setup recap from b.3.A

From `P16.1.b.3.A-first-residue.md` Theorem 10:

$$
\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) \;=\; \mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c,c,c,\delta)}(N) \;+\; 3\,R^{(4)}(N),\qquad 1/3 < \delta < 1-c,
$$

with

$$
R^{(4)}(N) \;=\; \kappa\,N\cdot\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^3}\int_{(c)^3}\!\! N^{s_1+s_2}\,\widehat\Psi(s_1)\widehat\Psi(s_2)\widehat\Psi(s_3)\widehat\Psi(1-s_3)\,G^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)\,\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)\,d^3 s,
$$

where

$$
\begin{aligned}
G^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3) \;&=\; \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^2\,\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_3)^2\,\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+1-s_3)^2\\
&\quad\cdot\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+s_3)^2\,\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+1-s_3)^2\,H(s_1,s_2,s_3,1-s_3),\\
\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3) \;&=\; \prod_{p \mid N^2+1}\!\bigl[\,1 + p^{-s_1}+p^{-s_2}+p^{-s_3}+p^{-(1-s_3)}\,\bigr].
\end{aligned}
$$

Constants: $\kappa = \pi/(8\,\zeta^{(3,4)}(2)) > 0$, $c = 1/2 + \epsilon_0$, $\epsilon_0 \in (0, 1/6)$, $\delta \in (1/3, 1-c) = (1/3, 1/2 - \epsilon_0)$. The function $H$ is holomorphic in $\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i) > 1/3$ and absolutely bounded there.

## 2. Pole structure of the $R^{(4)}$ integrand in $s_3$

Hold $(s_1, s_2)$ fixed with $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = \mathrm{Re}(s_2) = c$, generic imaginary parts. View the integrand of $R^{(4)}$ as a meromorphic function of $s_3$ alone. The factors carrying $s_3$ are:

| factor | pole in $s_3$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_3)$ at pole |
|---|---|---|
| $\widehat\Psi(s_3)$ | $s_3 = 0$ | $0$ |
| $\widehat\Psi(1 - s_3)$ | $s_3 = 1$ | $1$ |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_3)^2$ | $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ | $1 - c = 1/2 - \epsilon_0$ |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+1-s_3)^2$ | $s_3 = s_1$ | $c = 1/2 + \epsilon_0$ |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+s_3)^2$ | $s_3 = 1 - s_2$ | $1 - c = 1/2 - \epsilon_0$ |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+1-s_3)^2$ | $s_3 = s_2$ | $c = 1/2 + \epsilon_0$ |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^2$ | none in $s_3$ | — |
| $H(s_1,s_2,s_3,1-s_3)$ | none in $s_3$ for $1/3 < \mathrm{Re}(s_3) < 2/3$ | — |
| $\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)$ | none in $s_3$ for $\mathrm{Re}(s_3) > 0$ | — |

(For $H$: holomorphy of $H$ in $\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i) > 1/3$ from b.2 Theorem 8 forces $\mathrm{Re}(s_3) > 1/3$ AND $\mathrm{Re}(1-s_3) > 1/3$, i.e., $1/3 < \mathrm{Re}(s_3) < 2/3$. Our strip $[\delta, c] \subset (1/3, 2/3)$, so $H(s_1, s_2, s_3, 1-s_3)$ is holomorphic in $s_3$ throughout the strip. For $\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}$: each local factor $1 + p^{-s_1} + p^{-s_2} + p^{-s_3} + p^{-(1-s_3)}$ is an entire holomorphic function of $s_3$, never vanishing for real positive parameters; for complex $s_3$ in the strip $[\delta, c]$, $|p^{-s_3}|, |p^{-(1-s_3)}| < 1$ for $p \ge 2$, and uniform positivity follows for all but finitely many $p$ — the finite exceptional set being uniformly bounded.)

**Strip selection.** We want to shift $s_3$ from $\mathrm{Re}(s_3) = c$ to $\mathrm{Re}(s_3) = \delta$, where $1/3 < \delta < 1 - c$. Inside the strip $[\delta, c]$ lie:

- $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ at real part $1 - c \in (\delta, c)$ ✓
- $s_3 = 1 - s_2$ at real part $1 - c \in (\delta, c)$ ✓
- $s_3 = s_1$ at real part $c$ — **on the right boundary** ✗
- $s_3 = s_2$ at real part $c$ — **on the right boundary** ✗
- $s_3 = 1$ at real part $1 > c$ ✗
- $s_3 = 0$ at real part $0 < \delta$ ✗

The boundary cases $s_3 = s_1$ and $s_3 = s_2$ are problematic: a contour shift past a pole sitting on the contour is not well-defined. The next subsection introduces a contour deformation that removes them from the strip entirely.

## 3. Contour lift (the key new move)

**Lemma 15 (Stratified-contour regularization of $R^{(4)}$).** The 3-fold integrand of $R^{(4)}$ defined in b.3.A Theorem 10,

$$
F(s_1, s_2, s_3) \;=\; N^{s_1+s_2}\,\widehat\Psi(s_1)\widehat\Psi(s_2)\widehat\Psi(s_3)\widehat\Psi(1-s_3)\,G^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3)\,\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}(s_1,s_2,s_3),
$$

is meromorphic on the open polytope

$$
\Omega := \{(s_1, s_2, s_3) \in \mathbb{C}^3 \,:\, 1/3 < \mathrm{Re}(s_i) < 2/3 \text{ for } i = 1, 2; \; 1/3 < \mathrm{Re}(s_3) < 2/3\}
$$

with codim-1 pole hyperplanes

| factor | pole hyperplane | real-part equation |
|---|---|---|
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^2$ | $s_1 + s_2 = 1$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) + \mathrm{Re}(s_2) = 1$ |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_3)^2$ | $s_1 + s_3 = 1$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) + \mathrm{Re}(s_3) = 1$ |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+1-s_3)^2$ | $s_1 = s_3$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = \mathrm{Re}(s_3)$ |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+s_3)^2$ | $s_2 + s_3 = 1$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_2) + \mathrm{Re}(s_3) = 1$ |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+1-s_3)^2$ | $s_2 = s_3$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_2) = \mathrm{Re}(s_3)$ |

(No other factor — $\widehat\Psi$, $\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}$, $H$ — produces poles inside $\Omega$, as detailed in §2.)

**Claim.** For every $\eta \in (0, \eta_0)$ with $\eta_0 := \min(1/6 - \epsilon_0, \, 1 - c - \delta) > 0$, the symbol "$R^{(4)}$ on contour $(c)^3$" of b.3.A Theorem 10 should be interpreted as the integral on the **stratified contour**

$$
\mathcal{C}_\eta \;:=\; \{\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = c + \eta\} \times \{\mathrm{Re}(s_2) = c + \eta\} \times \{\mathrm{Re}(s_3) = c\}.
$$

This integral is absolutely convergent (the integrand is holomorphic in a tubular neighborhood of $\mathcal{C}_\eta$, since the real-part profiles satisfy $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) - \mathrm{Re}(s_3) = \eta > 0$ and $\mathrm{Re}(s_2) - \mathrm{Re}(s_3) = \eta > 0$, hence avoid the diagonal hyperplanes $s_1 = s_3$ and $s_2 = s_3$; and $\mathrm{Re}(s_1 + s_3) = 2c + \eta > 1$, etc.), and its value is independent of the choice of $\eta$ inside $(0, \eta_0)$ — by Cauchy's theorem applied iteratively in each $s_i$, since the homotopy $\eta \mapsto \eta'$ inside this range sweeps a domain in $\mathbb{C}^3$ free of polar hyperplanes.

*Justification.* In the original 4-fold integral $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) = (2\pi i)^{-4}\int_{(c)^4} I(\vec s) d^4 s$ of b.2 Theorem 7, the integrand $I$ is holomorphic on the contour $(c)^4$ since $\mathrm{Re}(s_i + s_j) = 2c > 1$ avoids all pole hyperplanes. After the $s_4$-residue extraction at $s_4 = 1 - s_3$ in b.3.A, the residual 3-fold integrand $F$ acquires new pole hyperplanes through the substitution: $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_4)^2 \mapsto \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + 1 - s_3)^2$ has pole at $s_1 = s_3$, similarly for $s_2$. On the contour $(c)^3$ (with $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = \mathrm{Re}(s_2) = \mathrm{Re}(s_3) = c$), these diagonal hyperplanes intersect the contour ON a codim-1 sub-contour ($\mathrm{Im}(s_1) = \mathrm{Im}(s_3)$ at the slice $s_1 = s_3$), which makes the literal "$(c)^3$" integral conditionally convergent at best.

The standard remedy is **stratification**: choose contours $(c+\eta, c+\eta, c)$ for $(s_1, s_2, s_3)$ where the real-part offsets place the diagonals strictly off the contour. The b.3.A Theorem 10 identity holds in the form

$$
\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) \;=\; \mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c, c, c, \delta)}(N) \;+\; 3\,R^{(4)}_{\mathcal{C}_\eta}(N),
$$

where $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c, c, c, \delta)}(N)$ is the 4-fold integral with only the $s_4$-contour shifted to $\mathrm{Re}(s_4) = \delta$ (the integrand $I(\vec s)$ is holomorphic on $(c, c, c, \delta)$ since the only pair-sum hyperplanes inside $[\delta, c]$ in $s_4$ are at $\mathrm{Re}(s_4) = 1-c \in (\delta, c)$, which are *crossed* by the shift, not *on* the new contour). The residual $R^{(4)}_{\mathcal{C}_\eta}$ is well-defined as the stratified-contour integral; by $S_3$-symmetry of the principal value on $(c)^3$, the three index choices $R^{(4)}_k$ for $k = 1, 2, 3$ each have the same value, computed on their own stratification $\mathcal{C}_\eta^{(k)}$ (where $s_k$ is the un-lifted variable). The factor of 3 is the $S_3$-orbit count.

**Clean derivation: lift in the 4-fold form before extracting the residue.** The cleanest way to make Lemma 15 rigorous is to perform the contour lift at the level of the *4-fold* integrand (which has no diagonal poles), and only THEN extract the $s_4$-residue. Concretely:

1. Start from $\mathcal{B}^\sharp(N) = (2\pi i)^{-4}\int_{(c)^4} I(\vec s)\,d^4 s$ of b.2 Theorem 7. The integrand $I$ has only pair-sum pole hyperplanes $\{s_i + s_j = 1\}$, real part $1 < 2c$, so $I$ is absolutely holomorphic on $(c)^4$ and on any contour with $\mathrm{Re}(s_i) > 1/2$ for all $i$.

2. **Lift $s_1, s_2$ in the 4-fold integrand from $(c)$ to $(c+\eta)$, keeping $s_3, s_4$ on $(c)$.** The strip $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) \in [c, c+\eta]$ in the 4-fold integrand contains only pair-sum poles at $\mathrm{Re}(s_1 + s_j) = 1$, requiring $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = 1 - \mathrm{Re}(s_j) \le 1 - c < c$. Outside the strip on the LEFT. Similarly for $s_2$. So the lift is unobstructed (no residues), and $\mathcal{B}^\sharp(N) = (2\pi i)^{-4}\int_{(c+\eta)^2 \times (c)^2} I(\vec s)\,d^4 s$.

3. **Now shift $s_4$ from $(c)$ to $(\delta)$ on this stratified contour.** The poles in $s_4$ are at $s_4 = 1 - s_k$ for $k = 1, 2, 3$, with real parts $1-c-\eta, 1-c-\eta, 1-c$ respectively. With $\delta < 1-c-\eta$ (built into $\eta_0$), all three are inside the strip $[\delta, c]$. By Cauchy:

$$
\mathcal{B}^\sharp(N) = \mathcal{B}^\sharp_{(c+\eta, c+\eta, c, \delta)}(N) + R^{(4)}_1 + R^{(4)}_2 + R^{(4)}_3,
$$

where each $R^{(4)}_k := (2\pi i)^{-3}\int_{(c+\eta)^2 \times (c)} \mathrm{Res}_{s_4 = 1-s_k} I(\vec s)\, d^3 s$.

4. **Equality of the three residues.** $R^{(4)}_3$ is on contour $\mathcal{C}_\eta = (c+\eta)^2 \times (c)$ with $s_3$ un-lifted. The residue integrand contains the diagonal factors $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + 1 - s_3)^2, \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - s_3)^2$ (poles at $s_1 = s_3, s_2 = s_3$); on $\mathcal{C}_\eta$, these poles sit at $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = \mathrm{Re}(s_2) = c \ne c+\eta$, i.e., OFF-contour. Integrand bounded on $\mathcal{C}_\eta$; $R^{(4)}_3$ absolutely convergent.

   Now consider $R^{(4)}_1$ on the SAME contour $(c+\eta)^2 \times (c)$: residue at $s_4 = 1-s_1$, residue integrand has diagonals $s_2 = s_1, s_3 = s_1$ (with pole at $\mathrm{Re}(s_2) = c+\eta = \mathrm{Re}(s_1)$ and $\mathrm{Re}(s_3) = c+\eta \ne c$). The first diagonal sits *on* the $(c+\eta)^2$-portion of the contour at $\mathrm{Re}(s_2) = c+\eta$ (codim-1 problem!). To avoid it, *relabel* the variables on $R^{(4)}_1$: rename $(s_1, s_2, s_3) \to (s_3', s_2', s_1')$ in the integration variable, then drop primes. This relabeling is just a change of dummy variable name; the integration domain becomes $(c) \times (c+\eta) \times (c+\eta)$, and the residue condition becomes $s_4 = 1 - s_3$ (since the new "$s_3$" was the old "$s_1$"). The relabeled integrand is $I$ evaluated on the relabeled variables, which by $S_3$-symmetry of $I$ in $(s_1, s_2, s_3)$ is the same function. So $R^{(4)}_1$ on $(c+\eta)^2 \times (c)$ = $R^{(4)}_3$ on $(c) \times (c+\eta)^2$ = $R^{(4)}_3$ on $\mathcal{C}_\eta$ (the latter by a further analytic-continuation lift exchanging the role of $s_1$ and $s_3$). The intermediate contour $(c) \times (c+\eta)^2$ has $\mathrm{Re}(s_2), \mathrm{Re}(s_3) = c+\eta$, $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = c$. The residue at $s_4 = 1 - s_3$ on this contour has diagonal poles at $\mathrm{Re}(s_2 + 1 - s_3) = 1$ and $\mathrm{Re}(s_1 + 1 - s_3) = 1$, requiring $\mathrm{Re}(s_2) = \mathrm{Re}(s_3) = c+\eta$ (degenerate, codim-2 in $\mathbb{C}^2$) and $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = \mathrm{Re}(s_3) - 0 = c+\eta - 0 = c+\eta$, contradicting $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = c$. So the residue integrand is holomorphic on $(c) \times (c+\eta)^2$, equal to its value on $\mathcal{C}_\eta = (c+\eta)^2 \times (c)$ by symmetry-of-$I$ + contour-permutation.

   Similarly $R^{(4)}_2 = R^{(4)}_3$. Hence $R^{(4)}_1 + R^{(4)}_2 + R^{(4)}_3 = 3 R^{(4)}_3 = 3 R^{(4)}_{\mathcal{C}_\eta}$.

5. **De-lift $s_1, s_2$ back down for the 4-fold residual:** $\mathcal{B}^\sharp_{(c+\eta, c+\eta, c, \delta)} = \mathcal{B}^\sharp_{(c, c, c, \delta)}$ by the same Cauchy argument as in step 2 (now de-lifting $s_1, s_2$ from $c+\eta$ to $c$, in the 4-fold integrand on the *new* contour with $s_4$ on $(\delta)$). No poles crossed (pair-sum hyperplanes at $\mathrm{Re}(s_1 + s_j) = 1$ outside the de-lift strip).

So:

$$
\mathcal{B}^\sharp(N) \;=\; \mathcal{B}^\sharp_{(c, c, c, \delta)}(N) \;+\; 3\,R^{(4)}_{\mathcal{C}_\eta}(N),
$$

with $R^{(4)}_{\mathcal{C}_\eta}$ on the stratified contour $\mathcal{C}_\eta = (c+\eta)^2 \times (c)$. This is the rigorous form of b.3.A Theorem 10; the literal "$R^{(4)}$ on $(c)^3$" symbol of b.3.A is *defined to be* $R^{(4)}_{\mathcal{C}_\eta}$.

**From now on** we interpret all instances of "$R^{(4)}$" as $R^{(4)}_{\mathcal{C}_\eta}$. $\Box$

**Consequence.** From now on, $R^{(4)}(N)$ is realized as

$$
R^{(4)}(N) \;=\; \kappa\,N\cdot\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^3}\int_{(c+\eta)\times(c+\eta)\times(c)}\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\![\text{same integrand}]\,d^3 s.
$$

(Same integrand; just contour relocated.) The pole structure in $s_3$ inside the strip $[\delta, c]$ is now:

| factor | $\mathrm{Re}(s_3)$ at pole | inside $[\delta, c]$? |
|---|---|---|
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_3)^2$ at $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ | $1 - c - \eta$ | **yes** (requires $\delta < 1 - c - \eta$, equivalently $\eta < 1 - c - \delta$; built into $\eta_0$) |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+s_3)^2$ at $s_3 = 1 - s_2$ | $1 - c - \eta$ | **yes** (same condition) |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+1-s_3)^2$ at $s_3 = s_1$ | $c + \eta$ | **no** (outside strip on right) |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2+1-s_3)^2$ at $s_3 = s_2$ | $c + \eta$ | **no** (outside strip on right) |
| $\widehat\Psi(1 - s_3)$ at $s_3 = 1$ | $1$ | **no** (outside strip on right) |
| $\widehat\Psi(s_3)$ at $s_3 = 0$ | $0$ | **no** (outside strip on left) |

(The bound $\eta < 1 - c - \delta$ is satisfied automatically since $\eta_0 = \min(1/6 - \epsilon_0, 1 - c - \delta) \le 1 - c - \delta$ and $\delta \in (1/3, 1-c)$ ensures $1 - c - \delta > 0$.)

**The lift accomplishes exactly what we wanted**: only the two original-type poles $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ and $s_3 = 1 - s_2$ remain strictly inside the strip; the diagonal poles are pushed out the right side. The contour shift in $s_3$ now crosses exactly two poles, not four.

## 4. Residue at $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ (Theorem 11)

**Theorem 11 (Residue at $s_3 = 1 - s_1$).** With $(s_1, s_2)$ on $(c+\eta)^2$ generic, the residue of the $R^{(4)}$-integrand at $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ is

$$
\mathrm{Res}_{s_3 = 1 - s_1}\!\Bigl[\widehat\Psi(s_3)\widehat\Psi(1-s_3)\,G^{(3)}\,\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}\Bigr]
\;=\; \kappa\,\widehat\Psi(1 - s_1)\,\widehat\Psi(s_1)\,\Gamma^{(3,1)}(s_1, s_2)\,\mathcal{D}_d^{(3,1)}(s_1, s_2),
$$

where

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Gamma^{(3,1)}(s_1, s_2) \;&:=\; \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^4 \cdot \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(2 s_1)^2 \cdot \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - s_1)^2 \cdot H(s_1, s_2, 1 - s_1, s_1),\\
\mathcal{D}_d^{(3,1)}(s_1, s_2) \;&:=\; \prod_{p \mid N^2+1}\!\Bigl[\,1 + p^{-s_1} + p^{-s_2} + p^{-(1-s_1)} + p^{-s_1}\,\Bigr] \\
&\phantom{:}\;=\; \prod_{p \mid N^2+1}\!\Bigl[\,1 + 2\,p^{-s_1} + p^{-s_2} + p^{-(1-s_1)}\,\Bigr].
\end{aligned}
$$

*Proof.* The factor $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_3)^2$ has a simple pole at $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ with residue $\kappa$ in the variable $s_3$ (Lemma 14 of b.3.A, with linear change $w = s_1 + s_3$, Jacobian $\partial w / \partial s_3 = 1$). Evaluating all other factors at $s_3 = 1 - s_1$:

- $\widehat\Psi(s_3) \mapsto \widehat\Psi(1 - s_1)$, $\widehat\Psi(1 - s_3) \mapsto \widehat\Psi(s_1)$.
- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_2)^2 \mapsto \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_2)^2$ (no $s_3$).
- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + 1 - s_3)^2 \mapsto \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + 1 - (1 - s_1))^2 = \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(2 s_1)^2$.
- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + s_3)^2 \mapsto \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - s_1)^2$.
- $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - s_3)^2 \mapsto \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - (1 - s_1))^2 = \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + s_1)^2 = \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_2)^2$.
- $H(s_1, s_2, s_3, 1 - s_3) \mapsto H(s_1, s_2, 1 - s_1, s_1)$.

Collecting the $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_2)^2$ factors (one from the explicit $G^{(3)}$ factor and one from the collapsing $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - s_3)^2$), we get $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_2)^4$. For $\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}$: $\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}(s_1, s_2, s_3)|_{s_3 = 1 - s_1} = \prod_{p \mid N^2+1}[1 + p^{-s_1} + p^{-s_2} + p^{-(1-s_1)} + p^{-(1-(1-s_1))}] = \prod_p[1 + p^{-s_1} + p^{-s_2} + p^{-(1-s_1)} + p^{-s_1}]$, since $1 - (1 - s_1) = s_1$. The two $p^{-s_1}$ terms combine to $2 p^{-s_1}$. $\Box$

**Structural observations.**

1. **Emergent $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^4$ double pole.** $\Gamma^{(3,1)}$ contains $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_2)^4$, which has a *double pole* at $s_1 + s_2 = 1$: expanding $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^2 = \kappa/(w-1) + \mathcal{R}(w)$, we get $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(w)^4 = \kappa^2/(w-1)^2 + 2\kappa\,\mathcal{R}(w)/(w-1) + \mathcal{R}(w)^2$. The next contour shift (in $s_1$ or $s_2$, across $\mathrm{Re}(s_1 + s_2) = 1$) will pick up a $(w-1)^{-2}$-type residue, which produces a $\log N$ factor when contracted with $N^{s_1 + s_2}$. This confirms the prediction of b.3.A §1: $\log N$ powers emerge from coalescences, here the coalescence of the $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^2$ factor with itself through the iteration.

2. **New simple pole at $s_1 = 1/2$.** $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(2 s_1)^2$ has a simple pole at $2 s_1 = 1$, i.e., $s_1 = 1/2$. The residue in $s_1$ is $\kappa/2$ (Jacobian $\partial(2 s_1)/\partial s_1 = 2$). Since $\delta < 1/2 < c + \eta$, this pole is INSIDE the next strip — it will be crossed by the $s_1$-shift.

3. **New anti-diagonal factor.** $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - s_1)^2$ has a pole at $s_1 = s_2$, at $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = c + \eta$ (on the upper boundary of the next strip $[\delta, c + \eta]$). Avoid by the same lift trick: lift the $s_2$-contour FURTHER, to $\mathrm{Re}(s_2) = c + 2\eta$, before shifting $s_1$. Then $s_1 = s_2$ pole is at $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = c + 2\eta$, outside $[\delta, c + \eta]$.

4. **$H$ at the residue.** $H(s_1, s_2, 1 - s_1, s_1)$ is holomorphic when $\min\{\mathrm{Re}(s_1), \mathrm{Re}(s_2), \mathrm{Re}(1-s_1), \mathrm{Re}(s_1)\} > 1/3$, i.e., $1/3 < \mathrm{Re}(s_1) < 2/3$ AND $\mathrm{Re}(s_2) > 1/3$. Our contour $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = c + \eta = 1/2 + \epsilon_0 + \eta \in (1/3, 2/3)$ for $\epsilon_0 + \eta < 1/6$, which is consistent with $\epsilon_0 \in (0, 1/6)$ and $\eta_0$ small. ✓

## 5. Symmetry: residue at $s_3 = 1 - s_2$ (Lemma 16)

**Lemma 16 ($S_2$-symmetry of the residue pair).** The 2-fold integrals

$$
R^{(4,k)}(N) \;:=\; \kappa^2\,N\cdot\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^2}\int_{(c+\eta)^2}\!\! N^{s_1+s_2}\,\widehat\Psi(s_1)\widehat\Psi(s_2)\widehat\Psi(1 - s_k)\,\widehat\Psi(s_k)\,\Gamma^{(3,k)}(s_1,s_2)\,\mathcal{D}_d^{(3,k)}(s_1,s_2)\,d^2 s
$$

for $k = 1$ and $k = 2$ (where $\Gamma^{(3,2)}, \mathcal{D}_d^{(3,2)}$ are defined analogously to Theorem 11 but with $s_1 \leftrightarrow s_2$ swapped throughout) are equal:

$$
R^{(4,1)}(N) \;=\; R^{(4,2)}(N) \;=:\; R^{(4,*)}(N).
$$

*Proof.* The integrand of $R^{(4)}$ is symmetric in $s_1 \leftrightarrow s_2$: each factor $N^{s_1 + s_2}, \widehat\Psi(s_1)\widehat\Psi(s_2), \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_2)^2$ is manifestly $S_2$-symmetric; the paired factors $(\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_3)^2, \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + s_3)^2)$ and $(\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + 1 - s_3)^2, \zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - s_3)^2)$ are exchanged by $s_1 \leftrightarrow s_2$; $H$ is $S_4$-symmetric (b.2 Lemma 12 in particular makes $H$ $S_4$-symmetric in $(s_1, s_2, s_3, s_4)$, hence symmetric in $s_1 \leftrightarrow s_2$); and $\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}$ is symmetric in $s_1 \leftrightarrow s_2$. The residue operation $\mathrm{Res}_{s_3 = 1 - s_k}$ commutes with the $s_1 \leftrightarrow s_2$ swap: residue at $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ becomes, under $s_1 \leftrightarrow s_2$, residue at $s_3 = 1 - s_2$, with the swapped integrand identical to the original (by symmetry). Hence

$$
R^{(4,1)}(N) \;=\; \int_{(c+\eta)^2}\mathrm{Res}_{s_3 = 1 - s_1}[\cdot]\,d^2 s \;\stackrel{s_1 \leftrightarrow s_2}{=}\; \int_{(c+\eta)^2}\mathrm{Res}_{s_3 = 1 - s_2}[\cdot]\,d^2 s \;=\; R^{(4,2)}(N).\quad\Box
$$

## 6. Contour-shift identity in $s_3$ (Theorem 12)

**Theorem 12 ($s_3$-contour shift of $R^{(4)}$).** For $1/3 < \delta < 1 - c$ and $0 < \eta < \eta_0 := \min(1/6 - \epsilon_0, \, 1 - c - \delta)$,

$$
R^{(4)}(N) \;=\; R^{(4)}_{\delta}(N) \;+\; 2\,R^{(4,*)}(N),
$$

where

$$
R^{(4)}_{\delta}(N) \;:=\; \kappa\,N\cdot\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^3}\int_{(c+\eta)^2 \times (\delta)}\!\! [\text{same integrand}]\,d^3 s,
$$

and $R^{(4,*)}(N)$ is the 2-fold residual integral of Lemma 16, explicitly:

$$
\boxed{\,
R^{(4,*)}(N) \;=\; \kappa^2\,N\cdot\frac{1}{(2\pi i)^2}\int_{(c+\eta)^2}\!\!\! N^{s_1 + s_2}\,\widehat\Psi(s_1)^2\,\widehat\Psi(s_2)\,\widehat\Psi(1 - s_1)\,\Gamma^{(3,1)}(s_1, s_2)\,\mathcal{D}_d^{(3,1)}(s_1, s_2)\,d^2 s.
\,}
$$

*Proof.* Fix $(s_1, s_2) \in (c+\eta)^2$ generic. The $s_3$-integrand on the strip $\mathrm{Re}(s_3) \in [\delta, c]$ has exactly two poles strictly inside the strip (per §3): $s_3 = 1 - s_1$ at $\mathrm{Re} = 1 - c - \eta$ and $s_3 = 1 - s_2$ at $\mathrm{Re} = 1 - c - \eta$. The other potential poles are outside the strip on the right ($s_3 = s_1, s_3 = s_2$ at $\mathrm{Re} = c + \eta > c$; $s_3 = 1$ at $\mathrm{Re} = 1 > c$) or left ($s_3 = 0$).

Apply Cauchy on the rectangle $\delta \le \mathrm{Re}(s_3) \le c$, $|\mathrm{Im}(s_3)| \le T$. The top and bottom edges contribute zero in the limit $T \to \infty$ by the rapid decay of $\widehat\Psi(s_3)\widehat\Psi(1 - s_3) \ll (1 + |\mathrm{Im}(s_3)|)^{-A}$ against the at-most-polynomial growth of the remaining factors (same argument as b.3.A §5, applied to two-variable polynomial growth of $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_k + s_3)^2$ on vertical lines, plus boundedness of $H$ and $\mathcal{D}_d^{(3)}$ on the strip).

By the residue theorem:

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\!\int_{(c)} (\text{integrand}) ds_3 \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\!\int_{(\delta)} (\text{integrand}) ds_3 \;+\; \mathrm{Res}_{s_3 = 1 - s_1}\![\cdot] + \mathrm{Res}_{s_3 = 1 - s_2}\![\cdot].
$$

Integrating over $(s_1, s_2) \in (c + \eta)^2$ (Fubini, justified by absolute convergence on each contour combination — the new contour $(c+\eta)^2 \times (\delta)$ has $\min\mathrm{Re}(s_i) = \delta > 1/3$, so $H$ is bounded, $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$ factors have polynomial growth, $\widehat\Psi$ gives rapid decay):

$$
R^{(4)}(N) \;=\; R^{(4)}_{\delta}(N) \;+\; R^{(4,1)}(N) + R^{(4,2)}(N) \;=\; R^{(4)}_{\delta}(N) + 2\,R^{(4,*)}(N),
$$

by Lemma 16. The explicit form of $R^{(4,*)}$ is the substitution from Theorem 11. $\Box$

## 7. Combined contour-shift identity

Combining b.3.A Theorem 10 (with $R^{(4)}$ interpreted as $R^{(4)}_{\mathcal{C}_\eta}$ per Lemma 15) and Theorem 12 of this chunk:

$$
\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}(N) \;=\; \mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c, c, c, \delta)}(N) \;+\; 3\,R^{(4)}_{\mathcal{C}_\eta}(N) \;=\; \mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c, c, c, \delta)}(N) \;+\; 3\,[\,R^{(4)}_\delta(N) + 2\,R^{(4,*)}(N)\,],
$$

so

$$
\boxed{\;\mathcal{B}^\sharp(N) \;=\; \mathcal{B}^\sharp_{(c, c, c, \delta)}(N) \;+\; 3\,R^{(4)}_\delta(N) \;+\; 6\,R^{(4,*)}(N)\;}
$$

— a 3-term decomposition. The first term is a 4-fold integral with $s_4$-contour shifted to $\mathrm{Re}(s_4) = \delta$; the second is a singly-residual 3-fold integral on $(c+\eta)^2 \times (\delta)$; the third is the doubly-residual 2-fold integral $R^{(4,*)}$ on $(c+\eta)^2$, whose analysis in the next chunk b.3.C will produce the first $\log N$ factor via the $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1+s_2)^4$ double pole.

The factor $6 = 3 \cdot 2$ is the product of (i) $S_3$-symmetry of the $s_4$-residue extraction over $(s_1, s_2, s_3)$ in b.3.A (factor 3), and (ii) $S_2$-symmetry of the $s_3$-residue extraction over $(s_1, s_2)$ here (factor 2). The cascading $S_3 \to S_2$ symmetry breaking is the cost of iterative residue extraction: at each stage, one variable is "consumed" by the residue condition, reducing the residual symmetry group.

## 8. Where this puts us: pole structure of $R^{(4,*)}$ in $(s_1, s_2)$

For the next chunk (b.3.C), we view $R^{(4,*)}(N)$ as a 2-fold integral in $(s_1, s_2)$ on $(c + \eta)^2$ and inventory its poles. The integrand contains:

| factor | pole locus in $(s_1, s_2)$ | $\mathrm{Re}$ at pole | order |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\widehat\Psi(s_1)$ | $s_1 = 0$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = 0$ | simple |
| $\widehat\Psi(s_2)$ | $s_2 = 0$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_2) = 0$ | simple |
| $\widehat\Psi(1 - s_1)$ | $s_1 = 1$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = 1$ | simple |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_1 + s_2)^4$ | $s_1 + s_2 = 1$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) + \mathrm{Re}(s_2) = 1$ | **double** |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(2 s_1)^2$ | $s_1 = 1/2$ | $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = 1/2$ | simple |
| $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}(s_2 + 1 - s_1)^2$ | $s_1 = s_2$ | (diagonal) | simple |
| $H, \mathcal{D}_d^{(3,1)}$ | none in $1/3 < \mathrm{Re} < 2/3$ region | — | — |

(For $\widehat\Psi(s_1)^2$ in $R^{(4,*)}$: $\widehat\Psi$ is a smooth-cutoff Mellin transform; near $s = 0$ it has a simple pole with residue $\Psi(0^+) - \Psi(\infty) = 1$ (b.2 Proposition 11). So $\widehat\Psi(s_1)^2$ has a *double* pole at $s_1 = 0$, which is OUTSIDE the next strip $[\delta', c + \eta]$ on the left and not crossed.)

For the next chunk, we want to shift, say, $s_1$ from $\mathrm{Re} = c + \eta$ down to $\mathrm{Re} = \delta'$ for some new target $\delta' \in (1/3, 1/2)$. Inside the strip $[\delta', c + \eta]$:

- $s_1 = 1 - s_2$: $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = 1 - c - \eta \approx 1/2 - \epsilon_0 - \eta$, INSIDE strip for $\delta' < 1 - c - \eta$. **Double pole (from $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^4$)**.
- $s_1 = 1/2$: $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = 1/2$, INSIDE strip for $\delta' < 1/2$. Simple pole.
- $s_1 = s_2$: $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = c + \eta$, ON upper boundary. Apply the lift trick again: lift $s_2$ to $\mathrm{Re} = c + 2\eta$ first, so $s_1 = s_2$ pole is at $\mathrm{Re}(s_1) = c + 2\eta > c + \eta$, outside.

So after a second lift ($s_2$ to $c + 2\eta$), the $s_1$-shift past $[\delta', c + \eta]$ encounters two poles: a double pole at $s_1 = 1 - s_2$ and a simple pole at $s_1 = 1/2$. The double pole produces $\log N$; the simple pole at $1/2$ gives $N^{1/2}$ from the residue.

This is exactly the calculation chunk b.3.C will do.

## 9. Sanity: $N$-power tally on the new contour

On the new contour $(c+\eta)^2 \times (\delta)$:

- $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c,c,c,\delta)}$ (first term): $\mathrm{Re}(\sum s_i) = 3c + \delta$, so the integrand size is $N^{3c + \delta}$. With $c = 1/2 + \epsilon_0$ and $\delta < 1/2 - \epsilon_0$: $3c + \delta < 3/2 + 3\epsilon_0 + 1/2 - \epsilon_0 = 2 + 2\epsilon_0$. Same scale as before the shift — but $\delta$ is significantly less than $c$, so this term is smaller than the naive estimate by a factor $N^{c - \delta}$.

- $R^{(4)}_\delta(N)$ (second term): residual carries $\kappa N$ outside, $N^{s_1 + s_2}$ inside on $(c + \eta)^2 \times (\delta)$. Total $N$-power scale $N^{1 + 2(c + \eta) + 0}$ from the integrand (the $\delta$-contour for $s_3$ contributes $N^{\mathrm{Re}(s_3)}$ inside the original integrand; but wait, $R^{(4)}$ has NO $N^{s_3}$ factor! It only has $N^{s_1 + s_2}$ — the $s_3$-integral is a "pure spectral" integral with no $N$-power. So $R^{(4)}_\delta(N)$ size $N^{1 + 2(c + \eta)} = N^{2 + 2\epsilon_0 + 2\eta}$.

- $R^{(4,*)}(N)$ (third term): explicit form has $\kappa^2 N \cdot N^{s_1 + s_2}$ on $(c+\eta)^2$. Total $N$-power $N^{1 + 2(c+\eta)} = N^{2 + 2\epsilon_0 + 2\eta}$. Same scale as $R^{(4)}_\delta$.

So all three terms are roughly $N^{2 + O(\epsilon_0)}$ before further analysis — too large by $N^2$ relative to the target $N^{o(1)}$. The reduction will come from:

1. Shifting $s_1, s_2$ in $R^{(4,*)}$ to $\mathrm{Re} = \delta'$, picking up the double-pole $\log N$ residue and simple-pole residues, recursively.
2. Bounding $\mathcal{B}^{\sharp}_{(c,c,c,\delta)}$ on the new contour $(c, c, c, \delta)$ via $|\mathcal{D}_d|_\infty \le 5^{\omega(N^2+1)}$ and the $\zeta_{\mathcal{Q}}^2$-product polynomial growth — yielding a bound of order $N^{3c + \delta} \cdot 5^{\omega(N^2+1)}$. With $\delta \to 1/3^+$ and $c \to 1/2^+$, $3c + \delta \to 11/6$. So this term is at most $N^{11/6 + o(1)} \cdot 5^{\omega(N^2+1)}$.

The dominant terms remain $R^{(4)}_\delta$ and $R^{(4,*)}$ until further shifts collapse them.
