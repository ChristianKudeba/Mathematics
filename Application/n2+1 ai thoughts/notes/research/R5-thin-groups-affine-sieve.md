# R5. Thin Groups and the Affine Sieve (Bourgain–Gamburd–Sarnak)

## 1. The setup

The **affine sieve** of Bourgain, Gamburd, and Sarnak ([BGS10], "Affine
linear sieve, expanders, and sum-product," Inventiones 179 (2010),
559–644, arXiv:0912.5021) takes as input:

- a finitely generated subgroup $\Gamma\subset \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$,
- an orbit $\mathcal{O}=\Gamma.v_0\subset \mathbb{Z}^n$,
- a polynomial $f\in\mathbb{Z}[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ that is "primitive" on
  $\mathcal{O}$ (the values $f(v),\,v\in\mathcal{O}$, have no fixed
  congruence obstruction).

The output is an **almost-prime** statement: there exist infinitely
many $v\in\mathcal{O}$ for which $f(v)$ has at most $R$ prime factors,
where $R=R(\Gamma,f)$ is effective but typically large. Counting
*genuine* primes is, in this generality, beyond reach, exactly because
Selberg / Brun-type sieves saturate at a bounded almost-prime level.

Three structural ingredients drive the proof:

1. **Combinatorial sieve** (Brun / Selberg upper bound) on the orbit
   $\mathcal{O}$, weighted by congruence conditions modulo squarefree
   $q\le Q$.
2. **Strong approximation** ([MVW84]): the reduction map
   $\Gamma\to \mathrm{SL}_n(\mathbb{Z}/q)$ is surjective on the
   Zariski-closure for almost all $q$.
3. **Superstrong approximation** = an effective spectral / expander
   bound: the family of Cayley graphs
   $\{\mathrm{Cay}(\Gamma/\Gamma(q),S\bmod q)\}_q$ forms an expander
   family. This gives a power-saving error term that the sieve needs.

The hard part is (3). It is what Bourgain–Gamburd
([BG08], "Uniform expansion bounds for Cayley graphs of
$\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$," Annals 167 (2008), 625–642) proved for
$\mathrm{SL}_2$: if $S\subset \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ is a finite
set whose Zariski closure is non-elementary (i.e. generates a
non-virtually-solvable subgroup), then $\mathrm{Cay}(\mathrm{SL}_2(p),S\bmod p)$
is a uniform expander as $p\to\infty$. Their proof combines a
flattening lemma based on the Helfgott "product theorem"
$\mathrm{SL}_2(p)$ with the Bourgain–Glibichuk–Konyagin sum–product
estimate and the Sarnak–Xue trick for converting flattening into a
spectral gap.

This was extended to arbitrary square-free moduli and to all
semisimple groups by Salehi-Golsefidy and Varjú
([SGV12], "Expansion in perfect groups," GAFA 22 (2012), 1832–1891,
arXiv:1108.4900), who proved super-approximation precisely when the
Zariski closure of $\Gamma$ has perfect connected component. This is
the strongest "superstrong approximation" theorem currently available.

## 2. What is a "thin group"?

A subgroup $\Gamma\subset \mathrm{SL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$ is **thin** if it
is Zariski dense but of infinite index in
$\mathrm{SL}_n(\mathbb{Z})\cap G(\mathbb{R})$ where $G$ is its Zariski
closure. Equivalently, $\Gamma$ "looks big" algebraically but is
"small" arithmetically — its orbit grows polynomially of degree
$<\dim G$ in some balls. The Bourgain–Kontorovich survey program is
the affine sieve applied to *thin* $\Gamma$, where one cannot use
classical automorphic-form input; superstrong approximation is the
substitute.

A standard textbook reference is the MSRI volume *Thin Groups and
Superstrong Approximation*, MSRI Publications 61 (2013), edited by
Breuillard and Oh, which collects survey articles by Sarnak, Lubotzky,
Varjú, Salehi-Golsefidy, Fuchs, Kontorovich.

## 3. Counting in a free monoid: the SL₂(ℕ₀) case

The free monoid $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N}_0)$ generated by
$S=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\1&1\end{pmatrix}$, $T=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$
is the *positive part* of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$. It coincides
with the monoid of matrices arising from finite continued-fraction
convergents (with positive partial quotients). It is the
"canonical thin sub-semigroup": it has Hausdorff dimension $1$ in the
limit set sense, it acts on $\mathbb{R}_{>0}$ by Möbius
transformations (the Stern–Brocot / Farey action), and the $k$-th
generation has $2^k$ elements while the $\le k$-th generation is
naturally indexed by binary strings.

Counting in such free monoids is the subject of Magee–Oh–Winter,
"Uniform congruence counting for Schottky semigroups in
$\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$," J. reine angew. Math. 753 (2019),
89–135 (arXiv:1601.03705), with appendix by Bourgain, Kontorovich,
and Magee. They prove a **uniform** asymptotic for the number of
elements of a Schottky semigroup of norm $\le N$ in each fixed
congruence class modulo $q$, with error term *uniform in* $q$. This is
the semigroup analogue of superstrong approximation, and it is exactly
the kind of input the affine sieve needs to attack thin orbits like
the CF semigroup. (See also Bourgain–Kontorovich–Magee, in the
appendix; and the SO(n,1) extension by Magee–Oh–Sarkar et al.,
arXiv:2108.00545.)

Two important caveats for **semigroups, not groups**:

- The standard Bourgain–Gamburd L²-spectral-gap proof uses the
  *symmetric* Cayley graph $S\cup S^{-1}$. In a free *monoid* one
  cannot invert. The substitute is the **transfer operator**
  $\mathcal L_s$ acting on holomorphic functions on a disc, whose
  spectral gap (a Dolgopyat-type estimate) plays the role of the
  expander gap. This is the route taken by Naud, Stoyanov,
  Magee–Oh–Winter.
- The "non-elementary" hypothesis on $S$ is replaced by:
  *the limit set has positive Hausdorff dimension and is not
  contained in a proper subvariety*. For our $\langle S,T\rangle$ free
  monoid the limit set is all of $[0,\infty]$, so $\delta=1$ — this is
  on the *boundary* of where Bourgain–Kontorovich–type techniques
  give the best bounds.

## 4. Sample applications

- **Apollonian curvatures**: positive density of integers
  appear as curvatures (Bourgain–Fuchs, JAMS 24 (2011), 945–967,
  arXiv:1001.3894). See R6.
- **Pythagorean hypotenuses in thin orbits**: Bourgain–Kontorovich,
  "Beyond Expansion I: Thin Hypotenuses," IMRN 2014 (arXiv:1307.3535),
  prove the level of distribution $\alpha<7/24$ for hypotenuses in a
  thin Pythagorean orbit, giving $R=4$ almost primes.
- **Almost-prime traces / Markov-type problems**:
  Bourgain–Kontorovich, "Beyond Expansion IV: Traces of Thin
  Semigroups," Discrete Analysis 2018 (arXiv:1310.7190), achieves
  $R=5$ almost primes in trace sets of CF semigroups, motivated by
  McMullen's arithmetic chaos conjecture for low-lying geodesics.
- **Almost-prime Pythagorean triples in thin orbits**:
  Kontorovich–Oh, J. reine angew. Math. 667 (2012), arXiv:0811.2236.

A useful survey is Kontorovich, "From Apollonius to Zaremba: local-
global phenomena in thin orbits," Bull. AMS 50 (2013), 187–228.

## Bridge to Shakov's framework

1. **Inputs are already in place for the BGS machine** at the level
   of *almost-primes*. The Magee–Oh–Winter uniform congruence
   counting theorem, applied to the free Schottky-like monoid
   $\langle S,T\rangle = \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N}_{>0})$, plus the
   Bourgain–Gamburd expansion for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$ with
   non-elementary generators (which $\{S\bmod p,T\bmod p\}$ certainly
   are, generating all of $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$ for every $p$),
   would in principle let one run the Brun sieve on the cross-term
   $\varphi(A) := ac+bd$ as a polynomial on the free monoid orbit.
   The expected output: there exist $A\in\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N})$
   such that $\varphi(A)$ has at most $R$ prime factors, with $R$
   effective. This says nothing yet about Shakov's complement
   problem.

2. **The Shakov characterization is a *negative* / *complement*
   statement, which the affine sieve does *not* directly address.**
   BGS produces almost-primes *in* the orbit; Shakov needs density of
   integers $n$ that are *not* in the image of $\varphi$. To convert,
   one would need: an asymptotic
   $\#\{A\in\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N}):\varphi(A)\le N\}\sim cN^\alpha$
   with $\alpha<1$, plus a sufficiently strong upper bound on the
   number of $A$ producing the same $n$. Then a positive proportion
   of $n\le N$ is missed. This is the "Zaremba-type" inversion of the
   sieve (cf. Bourgain–Kontorovich on Zaremba: density-1 of integers
   *are* in the image of denominators with bounded partial
   quotients).

3. **Relevant *quantitative* prediction.** The free monoid
   $\langle S,T\rangle$ has the property that an element of word-length
   $k$ has matrix entries that are sums of products of partial
   quotients; the expected size of $\varphi(A)$ for a random word of
   length $k$ grows like $\rho^k$ where $\rho>1$ is the spectral
   radius of the transfer operator. The number of words is $2^k$.
   Heuristically the *image* set has size $\asymp N^{\log 2/\log \rho}$,
   and so its complement in $[1,N]$ has positive density iff
   $\log 2/\log \rho<1$, i.e. $\rho>2$. For our case $\rho$ is
   essentially the top eigenvalue of the Gauss–Kuzmin–Wirsing
   transfer operator restricted to the alphabet $\{1,1\}$, and one can
   compute it explicitly — this is the analogue of
   Hensley's $\dim_H E_N$ calculation. (See R6 for the exact
   computation: in our setting the matrix-entry growth is governed by
   $\log\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2}$, the Fibonacci rate, so $\rho = \varphi^2$
   or thereabouts. Concretely, $\#\{A\in\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N}_0):
   \|A\|_\infty\le N\}\asymp N^2/\log N$ — same as $\mathrm{SL}_2$
   itself, by Mertens' formula. So the monoid is *not* "thin" with
   respect to the matrix norm; it is thin only with respect to
   *word-length*.)

4. **Cross-term polynomial $\varphi(A)=ac+bd$ is degree 2 and
   primitive** — exactly the regime where BGS-type sieving gives its
   sharpest results, and in fact $\varphi$ has the same algebraic
   shape as the Markov / trace polynomials studied in
   Bourgain–Kontorovich's "Beyond Expansion" series. Concretely,
   $\varphi(A) = \langle Av_1,Av_2\rangle$ where $v_1=(1,0),v_2=(0,1)$,
   so it is a quadratic form on the orbit $A\mapsto (Ae_1,Ae_2)$. This
   is structurally identical to the trace setup
   $\tau(A) = \langle Av,e_1\rangle + \langle Av,e_2\rangle$ for an
   appropriate $v$. The "Beyond Expansion IV" methods (additive-energy
   bounds for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$, Vinogradov-type sums) thus
   plausibly transfer, modulo replacing inversion-symmetric sums by
   one-sided ones.
