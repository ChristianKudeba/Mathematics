# R6. Zaremba's Conjecture and Apollonian Curvatures: Concrete Thin-Orbit Models

This file zooms in on the two best-developed thin-orbit programs —
Zaremba's conjecture for continued-fraction denominators, and prime
curvatures in integer Apollonian packings — and asks what each
suggests for Shakov's $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N}_0)$ problem.

## 1. Zaremba's conjecture

**Statement (Zaremba, 1971).** There is an absolute constant $A$ such
that for every integer $q\ge 2$ there exist $1\le p<q$ coprime to $q$
with $p/q = [0;a_1,\dots,a_k]$ all $a_i\le A$. Zaremba conjectured
$A=5$; Niederreiter $A=3$ for $q$ large.

Translate this into matrices: $p/q$ with partial quotients in
$\mathcal A=\{1,\dots,A\}$ corresponds to a product of matrices
$M_{a}=\begin{pmatrix}a&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ for $a\in\mathcal A$.
Set
$$
\mathcal G_{\mathcal A}\;=\;\langle M_a:a\in\mathcal A\rangle^+\;\subset\;\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z})_{\ge 0}.
$$
Then $q$ is a Zaremba-denominator iff $q$ is the $(2,1)$-entry of
some matrix in $\mathcal G_{\mathcal A}$. So Zaremba is a thin
*semigroup* orbit-coverage problem in
$\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})_{\ge 0}$ — a **direct cousin** of Shakov's.

**Bourgain–Kontorovich** ([BK14], "On Zaremba's conjecture," Annals
180 (2014), 137–196, arXiv:1107.3776) prove: for $A=50$, the set
$\{(2,1)\text{-entries of }\mathcal G_{\mathcal A}\}$ has **density 1**
in $\mathbb{N}$. The strategy:

1. **Hausdorff dimension of the limit set** of $\mathcal G_{\mathcal A}$.
   Define $E_A\subset[0,1]$ as the set of irrationals all of whose
   partial quotients lie in $\{1,\dots,A\}$. By
   Hensley ([Hen92], "Continued fraction Cantor sets, Hausdorff
   dimension, and functional analysis," J. Number Theory 40 (1992),
   336–358) and refinements (Jenkinson–Pollicott; Pollicott–Vytnova,
   arXiv:1611.09276 give $\dim_H E_2$ to >100 decimal places), the
   dimension $\delta_A=\dim_H E_A$ is computable as the unique $s$
   with $\lambda_s=1$, where $\lambda_s$ is the leading eigenvalue of
   the transfer operator
   $\mathcal L_s f(x) = \sum_{a\in\mathcal A}\frac{1}{(a+x)^{2s}}f\!\left(\frac{1}{a+x}\right).$
   Numerically $\delta_2 \approx 0.5312805062\ldots$, and
   $\delta_A\to 1$ as $A\to\infty$. The threshold $\delta>1/2$ is
   reached at $A=2$ already; the threshold $\delta>5/6$ (needed for
   Bourgain–Kontorovich) is reached around $A=50$.

2. **Hardy–Littlewood circle method on the orbit**, with the major
   arcs handled by superstrong approximation
   (Bourgain–Gamburd–Sarnak / Salehi-Golsefidy–Varjú expansion in
   $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$) and the minor arcs handled by
   bilinear / dispersion estimates that exploit the Cantor-set
   structure.

3. **Density-one rather than full conjecture**: the argument loses
   *some* integers because the major-arc analysis only captures the
   main term up to $o(N)$, not pointwise.

The "local-global" dream — that *every* sufficiently large $q$ in the
right congruence classes is hit — was recently shown to be **false** in
the analogous Apollonian setting (see §2) and partially false in the
CF setting too (Rickards, arXiv:2401.01860, Duke Math. J. 2025:
*Reciprocity obstructions in semigroup orbits in
$\mathrm{SL}(2,\mathbb{Z})$*, exhibits Hausdorff dimension
$1-\varepsilon$ semigroups inside
$\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})_{\ge 0}$ with quadratic-residue
obstructions on entries).

## 2. Apollonian prime curvatures

The **Apollonian group** $\mathcal A\subset O_F(3,1;\mathbb{Z})$
($F$ the Descartes form) is generated by 4 reflection involutions; it
is **thin** (Zariski dense, infinite index). An orbit of a Descartes
quadruple $v_0\in\mathbb{Z}^4$ records the curvatures of every circle
in the packing.

Three landmark results:

- **Sarnak's letter to Lagarias (2007)**: there are infinitely many
  circles of prime curvature in any primitive integer packing, and
  infinitely many tangent pairs of prime-curvature circles. Method:
  affine sieve (precursor of BGS10) + spectral gap of Lax–Phillips
  type for the Apollonian group acting on $\mathbb{H}^3$.

- **Bourgain–Fuchs ([BF11], JAMS 24 (2011), 945–967,
  arXiv:1001.3894)**: positive density of integers appear as
  curvatures in any primitive integer packing. They prove
  $\#\{n\le N:n\in\mathrm{curvatures}\}\gg N$ (matching the obvious
  upper bound up to a constant), settling the Graham–Lagarias–Mallows–
  Wilks–Yan positive-density conjecture.

- **Bourgain–Kontorovich ([BK14b], Inventiones 196 (2014),
  arXiv:1205.4416)**: density-1 (in admissible residue classes mod 24)
  of integers appear as curvatures — the *almost local-global*
  theorem.

- **Haag–Kertzer–Rickards–Stange (arXiv:2307.02749, Annals 200
  (2024))**: the **strict** Apollonian local-global conjecture is
  **false**. There exist quadratic and quartic obstructions (depending
  on quadratic reciprocity) that rule out infinite families of
  curvatures in specific packings. This was a major surprise.

The Bourgain–Kontorovich approach is again Hardy–Littlewood
circle-method-on-the-orbit: parameterize the orbit by group
generators, set up exponential sums $\sum_{\gamma:|\gamma v_0|\le N}
e(\alpha n(\gamma))$ where $n(\gamma)$ is the curvature, and bound the
minor arcs using *expansion in*
$\mathcal A/\mathcal A(q)\hookrightarrow O_F(\mathbb{F}_q)$ (a
superstrong-approximation theorem of Bourgain–Gamburd–Sarnak / SGV).

## 3. Common structure relevant to Shakov

Both programs (Zaremba and Apollonian) follow the same template:

| Step | Zaremba | Apollonian | Shakov $(ac+bd)$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Thin set $\Gamma$ | CF semigroup $\mathcal G_{\mathcal A}$ | Apollonian group $\mathcal A$ | $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N})$ free monoid |
| Polynomial $f$ | $(2,1)$-entry | curvature $\langle v_0, w\rangle$ | $\varphi(A)=ac+bd$ |
| Limit-set dim $\delta$ | $\delta_A\in(0,1)$ | $\approx 1.3057$ | **$\delta=1$** |
| Counting law | $\#\{|\gamma|\le N\}\asymp N^{2\delta_A}$ | $\asymp N^{\delta}$ | $\#\{|A|\le N\}\asymp N^2/\log N$ |
| Almost local-global | Density-1 (BK14) | Density-1 (BK14b) | **open** |
| Strict local-global | Obstructions (Rickards 2024) | False (HKRS 2024) | **probably false** |

Note that $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N})$ is the *full* CF semigroup (no
bounded-alphabet restriction), so $\delta=1$ — it is the densest of
all CF subsemigroups. This is both an advantage (more matrices, easier
to hit a given $n$) and a disadvantage (the methods of Bourgain–
Kontorovich need $\delta < 1$ for their dimension input to give
nontrivial savings; at $\delta=1$ one would expect every $n$ to be in
the image of $\varphi$ trivially — but Shakov shows this is exactly
*wrong* when restricted to the form $n^2+1$ being prime).

## 4. The transfer operator for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N}_0)$

For the *unrestricted* alphabet $\mathcal A=\mathbb{N}_{\ge 1}$, the
transfer operator is the Gauss–Kuzmin–Wirsing operator, whose top
eigenvalue is $1$ (it preserves the Gauss measure
$d\mu = \frac{1}{\log 2}\,\frac{dx}{1+x}$). The *second* eigenvalue
$\lambda_{\mathrm{GKW}} \approx 0.3036630029\ldots$ (Wirsing's
constant) controls the rate of equidistribution. This means any
"average" arithmetic statistic over $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N}_0)$
matrices of word-length $k$ converges at rate
$\lambda_{\mathrm{GKW}}^k$ to its limit.

For the *binary* alphabet $\mathcal A=\{1,2\}$, $\delta_2 \approx
0.5312$. The matrix-norm growth rate for a generic word of length $k$
is $\rho^k$ where $\log\rho = h(\mu)/\delta$ ($h$ is the entropy of
the appropriate Gibbs state). For Shakov's free monoid generated by
$S,T$ (which corresponds to alphabet $\{1\}$, i.e. all partial
quotients equal to $1$), the only words are $S^{a_1}T^{a_2}\cdots$,
and the matrix entries are products of *Fibonacci-like* sequences;
$\rho = \varphi^2 = (3+\sqrt5)/2$ where $\varphi$ is the golden ratio.

## Bridge to Shakov's framework

1. **Direct analogue of the Zaremba problem.** Shakov asks whether the
   image of $\varphi:\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N})\to\mathbb{N}$,
   $A\mapsto ac+bd$, is "almost everything" or has positive
   complement-density. By the theorem
   *"$n^2+1$ prime $\iff$ $n\notin\mathrm{im}\,\varphi$"*,
   Landau's 4th problem reduces to showing
   $|\mathbb{N}\setminus\mathrm{im}\,\varphi\,\cap[1,N]|\to\infty$. The
   Bourgain–Kontorovich Zaremba theorem gives the *opposite* direction
   for CF-denominators (density-1 are hit). For Shakov we want
   density-positive of $n$ are *missed* — the program would be:
   compute $\#\{A:\varphi(A)\le N\}$ asymptotically, then show that
   the *number of distinct values* it takes is $o(N)$ or exhibit
   strong concentration at composite $n$.

2. **Local-global is plausibly false for $\varphi$, in a
   *useful* way.** If $\varphi(A)=ac+bd$ and $ad-bc=1$, then
   $\varphi(A)^2 + 1 = (a^2+b^2)(c^2+d^2)$ (a known Diophantus-style
   identity used in Shakov's paper). So $\varphi(A)^2+1$ is *always*
   composite for $A\in\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N})$ with $A\ne I,S,T$.
   This is a **structural** obstruction completely analogous to the
   Haag–Kertzer–Rickards–Stange and Rickards reciprocity obstructions
   in Apollonian / CF orbits: the orbit is forced into the locus
   where $n^2+1$ factors as a sum-of-two-squares product, and the
   *complement* of that locus is exactly the primes
   $\equiv 1\pmod 4$ together with $2$. So the Shakov phenomenon is
   *the same flavor as* Rickards' reciprocity obstructions, but
   *positive* for our purposes: we *want* the obstruction, since the
   complement is what gives primes.

3. **Counting in the orbit is tractable.** Magee–Oh–Winter type
   uniform counting plus the Hensley/GKW transfer operator framework
   should allow asymptotic estimates
   $\#\{A\in\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N}):\varphi(A)\le N\}$. A naive
   guess: this count is $\asymp N\log N$ (matrices contribute
   roughly $|A|^2\sim\varphi(A)$, and the number of matrices with
   $|A|\le M$ is $\asymp M^2$, so the level set of $\varphi$ at $n$
   has size $\asymp\log n$). If true, the *average* fiber of
   $\varphi$ is $\asymp\log N$, the image has density $1/\log N$ —
   matching the prime number theorem density of $n$ such that $n^2+1$
   is prime. **This is a quantitative dictionary**: heuristically,
   $\#\{n\le N:\varphi^{-1}(n)=\emptyset\} \asymp N/\log N$, the
   prediction of Hardy–Littlewood for $\pi(N^2+1\text{ prime})$.

4. **Concrete sub-question for the BGS machinery.** Define
   $T(N) := \#\{A\in\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{N}_{>0}): \varphi(A)\le N\}$.
   By Magee–Oh–Winter (free-monoid version, requires the
   $\langle S,T\rangle$ Schottky setup) one can in principle prove an
   asymptotic $T(N) = c\, N\log N + O(N(\log N)^{1-\eta})$ with some
   power saving $\eta>0$. Combined with an *upper bound* on
   $\#\{A:\varphi(A)=n\}$ (a Diophantine-equation count, related to
   the divisor function $\tau(n^2+1)$ — exactly Shakov's 2025
   sequence A383066), this would give a quantitative count of
   $n\le N$ in the image, and the gap is exactly the prime count.
