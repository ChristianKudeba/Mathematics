# R8. Prime detection in automatic / k-regular sequences (Mauduit–Rivat and after)

## 1. The Mauduit–Rivat solution of Gelfond's problem

**Mauduit & Rivat, "Sur un probleme de Gelfond: la somme des chiffres des nombres premiers,"
*Annals of Math.* **171** (2010) 1591–1646**, doi:10.4007/annals.2010.171.1591. Settles a 1968
conjecture of Gelfond: for q >= 2 and gcd(d,q-1)=1, the sum-of-digits-in-base-q function s_q(p),
restricted to primes p, is equidistributed mod d; equivalently, sum_{p<=x} e(alpha s_q(p)) =
o(pi(x)) for alpha not in Z. Companion paper: Mauduit & Rivat, "La somme des chiffres des carres,"
*Acta Math.* **203** (2009) 107–148, did the same for squares (Gelfond's other digit conjecture).

**Methodology.** A Vaughan-type combinatorial decomposition splits sum_{p<=x} e(alpha s_q(p)) into
"type I" sums sum_{m,n} a_m e(alpha s_q(mn)) and "type II" bilinear sums; both are reduced to
exponential sums over n via van der Corput / Cauchy–Schwarz. The crucial novelty is the **carry
property**: |s_q(n+r) - s_q(n) - s_q(r)| (mod q-1) is controlled in terms of the number of carries,
which is rare for typical n. The carry lemma lets one truncate s_q(n) = s_q(n mod q^lambda) +
s_q(floor(n/q^lambda)) for a short window lambda << log x and treat the two pieces independently—a
"Fourier uniformity" condition on the truncated piece is what one actually verifies.

**Generalisations.**
- Drmota, Mauduit, Rivat, "Primes with an average sum of digits," *Compositio* **145** (2009)
  271–292 — extension to functions other than s_q.
- Drmota, Mauduit, Rivat, "Prime numbers in two bases," *Duke* **169** (2020) 1809–1876,
  arXiv:1611.10279 — joint distribution of digit sums in two coprime bases.
- Mullner, "Automatic sequences fulfill the Sarnak conjecture," *Duke* **166** (2017) 3219–3290,
  arXiv:1602.03042 — for *every* automatic sequence (a(n)) and the Mobius function mu, sum_{n<=x}
  mu(n) a(n) = o(x). This in particular gives a prime number theorem for any automatic sequence
  generated by a strongly connected automaton.
- Drmota, Mauduit, Rivat, "The Thue–Morse sequence along squares is normal," in preparation/JEMS;
  related: arXiv 2009.14773.
- Hanna & Spiegelhofer (and others), "Mobius orthogonality for q-semimultiplicative sequences,"
  hal-03919564 (2023).

These results all share a common feature: **the sequence in question takes values in a finite set** (a
finite alphabet, the unit circle, or a fixed cyclic group). The estimates are quantitative and produce
power savings of the form O(x^{1-eta}).

## 2. The carry property and what it requires

Define a sequence f: N -> C to satisfy the **carry property at level alpha in (0,1)** if there is
eta > 0 such that for all lambda >= 1 and almost all n < q^lambda, all r < q^lambda with n+r <
q^lambda,

  f(n+r) = f_lambda^{trunc}(n+r) + (small),  with |{n: f(n+r) != ...}| <= q^{lambda(1-eta)}.

For automatic sequences, this is established because the automaton's state after reading the high
digits of n is unaffected by adding a small r—provided the automaton has bounded memory. **The carry
property fails generically for k-regular sequences** that are not automatic, because their values are
unbounded: small changes to low digits can multiply the matrix product A_{d_0}...A_{d_L} by something
large in the L-infinity norm even when most digits are unchanged. Concretely, for an unbounded
k-regular f, one cannot bound |f(n+r) - f(n)| by anything uniform in n, only by something growing in
n. This is the principal obstacle to a Mauduit–Rivat theorem for unbounded k-regular sequences.

## 3. Sarnak / Chowla and the bounded-values dichotomy

Mullner's theorem (Duke 2017) and the survey "Some recent developments on the Sarnak conjecture"
(Drmota et al., dmg.tuwien.ac.at/drmota/survey.pdf) make the bounded-values requirement explicit:
the methods produce orthogonality of any bounded automatic (a(n)) with mu(n). The "boundedness" is
used (i) to reduce L^2 sums to pointwise digit sums via Cauchy–Schwarz, (ii) to apply discrete
Fourier on a finite alphabet, and (iii) to get the trivial estimate |sum a(n) mu(n)| <= ||a||_infty
pi(x). All three break for unbounded f. Some workarounds:

- **Truncate**: study f(n) · 1_{f(n)<=T} and let T grow slowly with x. Loses the algebraic structure.
- **Normalise**: study f(n)/E[f] on a dyadic block; works for sequences with sub-polynomial growth and
  controlled large-deviations (e.g., Stern; here Heuberger–Krenn fluctuations help).
- **Use the linear representation directly**: replace the automaton states by the rank-d matrix
  product A_{d_0}...A_{d_L}; since these matrices have bounded operator norm only if the joint
  spectral radius rho < 1, this works only in the contractive regime—not generally.

A relevant recent paper: Konieczny, "Mobius orthogonality for q-semimultiplicative sequences,"
*hal-03919564* (2023), extends some Mauduit–Rivat machinery to a broader class than automatic, but
still under boundedness.

## 4. Detecting primes (rather than just averaging) inside a regular sequence

The Mauduit–Rivat methodology averages a fixed sequence along primes; it does *not* directly tell you
whether a given value is taken by a prime. Two relevant strands:

- **Friedlander–Iwaniec, "The polynomial X^2+Y^4 captures its primes," Annals 1998**: a true
  "primes-in-thin-set" theorem. Uses asymptotic sieve for primes. Iwaniec 1978 (*Invent. Math.* 47)
  shows n^2+1 is prime or semiprime infinitely often—the closest standing approach to Landau IV.
- **Sieve in two dimensions** (Heath-Brown's x^3 + 2y^3, etc.): produces primes in sparse algebraic
  sets when sufficient analytic input (level of distribution >= 1/2) is available.

For a 2-regular sequence S, asking "is S(k) prime infinitely often" or "does S(k) hit infinitely many
primes" is an entirely different problem from Gelfond-type equidistribution. There is essentially no
literature on prime detection inside the *image* of a k-regular sequence — this is a genuine gap.

## Bridge to Shakov's framework

1. **Mauduit–Rivat does not directly apply to S.** S is unbounded (S(2^n) = n is the slowest sub-
   sequence, but S(2^n - 1) and intermediate values can be much larger), so the carry property in its
   standard form fails. Specifically, if k = 2^n then S(k) = something small, while S(k+1) = S(2^n+1)
   has been computed by the recursion to grow in a very different pattern—any uniform Lipschitz-style
   carry bound would prevent the |S^{-1}(n)| = tau(n^2+1) identity, since that identity forces wild
   fluctuations. So a direct adaptation of Mauduit–Rivat to S is blocked.

2. **However, the dual question may be tractable**: instead of asking about primes among S(k), one can
   ask about the *equidistribution of s_q(p)* with q chosen so that base-q digit-sums are linked to S
   (perhaps via the linear representation of S). If a "twisted Gelfond" theorem
   sum_{p<=x} e(alpha S(p)) = o(pi(x)) could be proven, it would give nontrivial information about how
   often S(p) lies in arithmetic progressions—which interacts with the Landau IV question through the
   |S^{-1}(n)| = tau(n^2+1) identity (since divisor functions of n^2+1 over primes-by-S relate to the
   problem). This requires extending Mauduit–Rivat's carry/Fourier framework to handle the unbounded
   linear-representation-output sums; see §3 above. The matrix L = I + N (single Jordan block with
   N^3 = 0) means digit-changes propagate polynomially in window length, **so a "carry property with
   polynomial loss" might still suffice** — concretely, |S(n+r) - S(n)| = O((log n)^2) when r is
   small, which is much better than generic k-regular sequences and may bring the problem within reach
   of a Drmota–Mauduit–Rivat-style approach.

3. **Mullner-Sarnak for the indicator 1_{S(k)=n}.** For each fixed n, the set { k : S(k) = n } has
   cardinality tau(n^2+1) by hypothesis. The indicator 1_{S(k)=n} is *not* automatic in k (it is a
   level set of a k-regular sequence). But one might hope it is "close to automatic" in some
   quantitative sense; if so, Mullner's Sarnak result would give sum_{k<=x} mu(k) 1_{S(k)=n} = o(x),
   which—combined with the Landau identity—would translate into nontrivial information about
   sum mu(k) over k mapping to n. Whether this is useful for proving infinitude of primes p with
   p^2+1 prime is unclear; it more naturally constrains the *distribution* of preimages.

4. **A negative observation.** Even a complete Mauduit–Rivat-style theorem for S — say,
   sum_{p<=x} e(alpha S(p)) = o(pi(x)) for alpha irrational — would *not* by itself resolve Landau IV.
   Such a theorem says S(p) equidistributes; Landau IV requires existence of infinitely many n with
   tau(n^2+1) = 4, i.e., |S^{-1}(n)| = 4. Any prime-distribution input to Shakov's framework feeds in
   as a *constraint* on fiber sizes, not a direct count. The right Mauduit–Rivat analogue would be a
   statement about preimages: "infinitely many primes p have |S^{-1}(S(p))| = 4," which is a much more
   delicate question requiring sieving on the *codomain* of S.
