# Equivariant maps $SL_2(\mathbb{N}_0) \to \mathcal{D}_f$

## The condition

$F : SL_2(\mathbb{N}_0) \to \mathcal{D}_f$ is **equivariant** if
$$F(SA) = \bar S(F(A)) \quad\text{and}\quad F(TA) = \bar T_f(F(A))$$
for all $A$.

Equivalent (Prop `prop_equivariant`): $F(SA) = \bar S F(A)$ and $F(c(A)) = \bar c_f F(A)$, where $c$ is the [[05-complement-involution|complement involution]]. This is more useful in proofs — the complement gives you $\bar T_f$ automatically.

## Determined by F(I)

Because [[01-sl2-n0-monoid|SL₂(ℕ₀) is freely generated by $S, T$]], an equivariant $F$ is **determined by $F(I)$**. Conjugation by the complement, plus the boundary conditions, force $F(I) = (1, 0)$ in the invertible case (Lemma `lemma_F(I)=(1,0)`), and then $F$ is unique.

This is why we name it $\hat F_f$.

## Two key examples — the families Φ and Ψ

For $\beta \in \mathbb{N}_0$:

$$\Phi_\beta\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} = (a^2 + \beta ab + b^2,\ ac + \beta bc + bd)$$

For $\psi_\beta(n) = n^2 + \beta n - 1$:
$$\Psi_\beta\begin{pmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{pmatrix} = (\max\{a,b\}^2 + \beta ab - \min\{a,b\}^2,\ \max\{ac,bd\} + \beta bc - \min\{ac,bd\})$$

(The max/min arises because some signs would otherwise leave ℕ₀.)

These are equivariant — *check* by computing both sides on $SA$ and $c(A)$.

## Equivariance is the rigid skeleton

This is the crucial observation: **equivariance forces the entire map**. We don't have freedom in defining $\hat F_f$ — once we say $\hat F_f(I) = (1,0)$, everything is determined recursively by $\bar S, \bar T_f$.

So **invertibility of $\hat F_f$ is a property of $f$**, not a choice. Either it works (enumerable) or it doesn't.

## Why the conditions cut so hard

For $\hat F_f$ to be a *bijection*:
- **Injective**: every divisor pair $(m,n)$ has a *unique* "decomposition" using only $\bar S, \bar c_f$ that reduces it to $(1,0)$. Like a unique continued fraction.
- **Surjective**: every $(m,n) \in \mathcal{D}_f$ is reachable. Like every rational has a CF expansion.

Proposition `maincondition` translates this into the bound:
$$\min\{m, |f(n)|/m\} \le n < \max\{m, |f(n)|/m\}$$
for every $(m, n) \in \mathcal{D}_f \setminus \{(1,0)\}$.

This is a **size constraint on divisor pairs**, and it is what cuts the eligible polynomials down to four ([[07-classification-theorem]]).

## Connection to continued fractions

Iterating $\bar S^{-1}$ and $\bar c_f$ to reduce $(m,n) \to (1,0)$ is structurally identical to the **Euclidean algorithm / continued fraction expansion** of $n/m$ — except the "alternation" is enforced by the $\bar c_f$ step (you can't apply $\bar S^{-1}$ twice without a $\bar c_f$ in between *unless* the $\bar c_f$ would leave $\mathcal{D}_f$).

See [[17-inversion-algorithm]] for the explicit algorithm and [[16-calkin-wilf-tree]] for the closest non-divisor analogue.
