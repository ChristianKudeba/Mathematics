## What I did

1. **Fourier expansion.** $\widetilde S_h(e) = \prod_{p \mid e} 2\cos(2\pi h a_p^{(e)}/p)$.
   For $p = 2$: both signs $\epsilon_2 \cdot 1/2 \equiv 1/2 \pmod 1$ collapse,
   so $p = 2$ contributes a single character of frequency $1/2$, NOT two.
   Hence $\widetilde S_h(e) = \sum_\epsilon e^{2\pi i h \tilde\alpha_\epsilon(e)}$
   where the sum runs over $\epsilon \in \{\pm 1\}^{\omega'(e)}$, with
   $\omega'(e) := \omega(e) - [2 \mid e]$. (My initial draft used
   $2^{\omega(e)}$; skeptic round-1 effectively forced this correction.)

2. **Lemma F.1 (no torsion).** For sf-good $e_1 \ne e_2$ and any sign
   vectors, $\beta := \tilde\alpha_{\epsilon_1}(e_1) + \tilde\alpha_{\epsilon_2}(e_2)
   \not\equiv 0 \pmod 1$. Case A (odd prime in $u_1 := e_1/\gcd$) — that
   prime's component has denominator $p$, can't cancel. Case B
   ($u_1 = 2$, the only-power-of-2 case) — $\beta \equiv 1/2 + r/Q$ with
   $Q$ odd; $\equiv 0$ would require $2r + Q \equiv 0 \pmod{2Q}$ but
   $2r + Q$ is odd, contradiction.

3. **Theorem F.2 ($R(N, H) \to 1$ as $H \to \infty$).** Three steps:
   (i) numerator off-diagonal bounded by $C_{\mathrm{num}}(N)$ via geometric
   series + Lemma F.1; (ii) denominator $\sum_h D_h = H \cdot A(N) + O_N(1)$
   where $A(N) = \sum_{e \le N, \mathrm{sf,good}} 2^{\omega'(e)}$ — derived
   by expanding $D_h(e) = \widetilde S_h(e)^2$ as a $(\epsilon_1, \epsilon_2)$
   double sum, identifying $\gamma \equiv 0$ pairs as $\epsilon_2 = -\epsilon_1$
   on odd primes ($p = 2$ contributions cancel: $1/2 + 1/2 \equiv 0$);
   (iii) combine for $|R - 1| \le C(N)/H$, $C(N) = 2 C_{\mathrm{num}}/A$.

4. **Empirics.** `bot/scratch/Halasz-Fourier-cesaro.py` (chunked vectorized
   $h$-loop). At $N \in \{10, 20, 50, 100\}$, $H$ up to $10^6$:
   - $N = 10$: $R = 1.000000$ exactly already at $H = 100$ (period $\mathrm{lcm} = 10$ divides $H$).
   - $N = 20$: $|R - 1|$ goes $0.054 \to 0.0018 \to 3.7 \cdot 10^{-4} \to 3.8 \cdot 10^{-5} \to 6.7 \cdot 10^{-6}$ at $H = 10^2, \ldots, 10^6$.
   - $N = 50$: $|R - 1|$ goes $0.035 \to 0.0046 \to 0.0022 \to 6.9 \cdot 10^{-5} \to 4 \cdot 10^{-6}$.
   - $N = 100$: $|R - 1|$ goes $0.030 \to 0.034 \to 8.4 \cdot 10^{-4} \to 6.5 \cdot 10^{-6} \to 1.5 \cdot 10^{-5}$ — non-monotone, but always within an $O(1/H)$ envelope.
   - $A(N)$-formula verified to 5 digits: $A(20) = 9$, $A(50) = 19$, $A(100) = 43$.

5. **Strategic conclusion.** Theorem F.2 says: prev-session hint #1's
   suggestion (orthogonality gives a NEGATIVE bound $\sum_h(|H_h|^2 - D_h) \le 0$)
   is wrong-direction. Orthogonality gives the Cesaro mean = $0$, not
   $\le -c$. Empirical pre-Cesaro negativity at moderate $H$ comes from
   per-$h$ cancellation $|H_h|^2 \ll D_h$, which IS the strategic goal.
   Crossover $H_*(N) \sim \mathrm{lcm}(\text{primes }\equiv 1 \pmod 4 \le N)$
   is superexponential in $N$ — wrong limit for the strategic problem.

## Result

**Theorem F.2 (rigorous).** For each fixed $N \ge 2$,
$$
R(N, H) := \frac{\sum_{h=1}^H |H_h(N)|^2}{\sum_{h=1}^H D_h(N)}
\xrightarrow[H \to \infty]{} 1, \quad
|R(N, H) - 1| \le C(N) / H,
$$
with $C(N) = 2 C_{\mathrm{num}}(N) / A(N)$ explicit (finite, $N$-dependent).
Proof via Lemma F.1 (no-torsion: for sf-good $e_1 \ne e_2$, no
$(\epsilon_1, \epsilon_2)$ gives $\beta \equiv 0 \pmod 1$) plus
geometric-series numerator bound plus exact denominator main-term
identity.

**Empirical verification.** $A$-formula matches $\sum_h D_h / H$ to 5
digits at $N = 20, 50, 100$. $|R - 1|$ data consistent with $O_N(1/H)$
envelope.

**Strategic conclusion.** The prev-session hint #1 ("$\sum_h(|H_h|^2 - D_h) \le 0$
as Fourier identity over $h$") is RESOLVED in the negative direction.
$h$-orthogonality alone gives $\sum_h(|H_h|^2 - D_h) = O_N(1)$
as $H \to \infty$, NOT $\le -c$. The empirical pre-Cesaro negativity at
moderate $H \ll \mathrm{lcm}(\text{relevant primes})$ comes from per-$h$
cancellation $|H_h(N)|^2 \ll D_h(N)$, which IS the strategic goal
$|H_h(N)| \ll \sqrt N$. Theorem F.2 is rigorous but at the wrong limit:
$H \to \infty$ at fixed $N$, while we need fixed (small) $H$ at $N \to \infty$.

**Rigor accounting.** This is the FIRST rigorous theorem in the
Halász-$h$-thread (sessions 2026-05-08 onward). All prior notes were
empirical or heuristic. Modest in scope (the limit is at the wrong
direction for the strategic goal) but the negative strategic conclusion
is durable — it definitively closes one candidate analytic route.
