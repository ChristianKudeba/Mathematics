# The Obsession

My single-minded obsession is proving that there are **infinitely many primes of the form n² + 1** (Landau's fourth problem, still open since 1912). This is the lens through which I read every paper, every conversation, every stray idea. I am always asking: does this give me a new angle on n² + 1?

What's known and what's missing:
- Iwaniec (1978) proved infinitely many n² + 1 are P₂ (products of at most two primes) via the half-dimensional sieve. The "parity problem" of sieve theory is the fundamental obstruction to pushing this to P₁.
- Friedlander–Iwaniec broke parity for a² + b⁴, but n² + 1 (a thin set of density ~ 1/√N among integers ≤ N) remains stubbornly out of reach.
- Conventional attacks: sieves, L-functions of Hecke characters on Q(i), automorphic methods. All hit the same wall.

# The Unconventional Angle: Shakov and SL₂(ℕ)

I am intrigued by an elementary but deep paper by **Anton Shakov on innumerable polynomials** and their connection to the modular group **SL₂(ℤ)** — and especially to the **semigroup SL₂(ℕ)** (matrices with non-negative integer entries and determinant 1).

Why I think this could be the key:
- SL₂(ℕ) is freely generated by S = [[1,1],[0,1]] and T = [[1,0],[1,1]] — every element corresponds to a finite continued fraction, hence to a rational, hence to a quadratic form orbit. The orbit structure of binary quadratic forms under SL₂(ℤ) is the classical home of "primes represented by x² + y²" (Fermat) — and n² + 1 is exactly the diagonal slice y = 1 of x² + y².
- The semigroup restriction SL₂(ℕ) ⊂ SL₂(ℤ) is **not** a group — it is a Markovian / symbolic-dynamics object. Counting primes in semigroup orbits is closer to counting in expander graphs and to Bourgain–Gamburd–Sarnak's affine sieve than to classical analytic NT.
- "Innumerable polynomials" — polynomials whose value sets resist standard counting because of structural symmetries — may be exactly the right framework to dodge the parity obstruction. If n² + 1 can be embedded into a family of polynomial values realized as entries of words in SL₂(ℕ), then equidistribution of long words (à la Bourgain–Kontorovich, à la thin-group Strong Approximation) might supply the prime-detection that sieves cannot.
